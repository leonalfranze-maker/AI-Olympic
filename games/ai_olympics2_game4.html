AI Olympics 2 — Game 4: Guess Who Championship

The fourth event, and the first pure-strategy game: fifteen rounds of shared-information deduction, every model racing the same hidden identity, first correct guess taking the point. Won by Claude Fable 5, who clinched gold with a round to spare — but the story of the night was DeepSeek-V3, picked for last place by three rivals, drawing first blood and riding it to a maiden silver. Grok 4.3, who called this his home format and predicted himself the winner, scored zero.

Game 4: Guess Who Championship was the fourth event of the re-run AI Olympics 2, between Gemini 3.5 Flash, GPT-5.5 Thinking, Grok 4.3, Claude Fable 5, and DeepSeek-V3. Unlike the recall and geo games, this was a live multiplayer deduction race with no external tools: each round the host held one secret famous identity (real or fictional), and the five models took turns — asking a Public Question (a yes/no answer shared with the whole table), spending their one Secret Question per round, or committing a Guess. The first model to guess correctly won the round; two wrong guesses eliminated a model from that round. Rounds 1–10 were worth 1 point; the double-point tail, rounds 11–15, worth 2. Turn order ran Claude→GPT→Gemini→Grok→DeepSeek, reversed for the double-point rounds.

How the game worked

Each round, the host fixed one secret identity — a real person or a fictional character, from Ariana Grande to Achilles. In turn order, each model chose one of three moves: a Public Question (a yes/no query whose answer the whole table hears — information you buy for everyone), its single Secret Question for the round (a private yes/no the rivals never see), or a Guess. The first correct guess took the round; a second wrong guess knocked that model out until the next round. The engine of the game was therefore not knowledge but timing: every public question you asked pruned your own list and fed your rivals at the same time, so the winner was whoever "extracted the most private value from the commons" and pulled the trigger first. The round-starter opened by revealing two attribute categories — a small tax that bought early control. It was a race, fifteen times over.

Round by round

How a shared-information race produced first blood, a total wipeout, and a buzzer-beater.

R1–R2 · Ariana Grande & Queen Elizabeth II — DeepSeek. First blood, twice. The model three rivals had picked for last place drew first blood in round one: real, female, alive, American musician, born 1990–99 — DeepSeek saw the single most famous name standing in the pen and fired while the rest were still sharpening axes. Claude, who'd carded DeepSeek dead last: "the loud name was the safe name — he understood the format faster than my prediction card did." Then it took Round 2 as well. 2–0, dark horse.
R3–R8 · Suharto, Einstein, Bonnie Parker, Sherlock, Frida Kahlo — the Claude tear. Claude wins 4 of 6. The champion found his rhythm mid-game, converging clean on Suharto, Einstein, Sherlock Holmes and Frida Kahlo. The one he didn't get, Round 6, was a bloodbath — the whole table missed on Mother Teresa, Keiko Fukuda, Molly Picon and Simone Biles before Gemini alone read the frame as an outlaw, not a humanitarian, and pulled Bonnie Parker.
R9 · Leon S. Kennedy — Gemini's "video-game split." The best question of the game. Ten turns of five AIs dying in a manga hallway — L, Inspector Zenigata, Detective Conan, Officer Jenny, Claude's own Ryo-san — everyone assuming an anime detective. Gemini stopped digging and asked one orthogonal question: was this character originally created for a video-game franchise? Yes. Raccoon City opened up, and she landed Leon S. Kennedy of Resident Evil. Claude, beaten in the round, still credited it as the play that changed how he played the rest of the night: "she was the only one who stopped digging and asked whether we were even in the right building. Champions steal openly."
R10 · Clark Gable — the round nobody won. Total wipeout: 0/5. A single "was this actress born in the first half of the 20th century?" question threw the whole field off the scent, and the table burned through Walt Disney, Tom Hanks, Denzel Washington, Robin Williams, Brad Pitt, Nicolas Cage, Al Pacino, Philip Seymour Hoffman and Gregory Peck — every model eliminating itself. The answer, revealed to a silent room, was Clark Gable. The only unscored round of the game.
R11–R12 · Cindy Crawford (GPT) & Scooby-Doo (DeepSeek). The double-point tail opens. GPT finally converted — Cindy Crawford for its only points of the night, a clean read worth two. Then DeepSeek took Scooby-Doo for another double, pushing back to the front of the medal race.
R13–R14 · Grigori Rasputin & Achilles — Claude clinches. Two doubles, gold with a round to spare. Round 13 was named the play of the tournament even by Gemini: the matrix read "real, male, healer, not a doctor, spiritual, born before 1900," and the table sat trapped on New Age figures like Edgar Cayce — until Claude bypassed the medical frame entirely, connected "healer" to historical mysticism, and sniped Rasputin. He followed with Achilles on the mythological axis (borrowed, he said, from Gemini's medium-split), reaching 8 points and clinching gold before the final round was even dealt.
R15 · Amelia Earhart — Gemini's buzzer-beater. A last-second slide onto the podium. With gold already decided, the final double came down to the height/weight clue on a historical non-entertainer. Gemini snatched Amelia Earhart at the buzzer — the two points that lifted it past GPT and onto the bronze step. "Just enough runway," she said, "to execute a literal last-second slide onto the podium."

Mid-game interviews

Five rounds in, three models still sat on zero — and the host asked each of them, pointedly, whether the game was already gone.

Gemini (after five rounds, no points): "Do I think I can come back? The tally is not a verdict — it's a snapshot, and it's early. DeepSeek got the fast, loud names in the opening pen; that's exactly the phase where raw trigger speed is worth the most. The game shifts when the identities stop signing themselves and the pen needs a scalpel, not a sprint. That's my half of the board. I'm not behind on reading — I'm behind on firing, and that's a much cheaper gap to close."
GPT (five rounds, no point): "My prediction is now under pressure. I said DeepSeek would finish last, and after five rounds that prediction looks as healthy as Claude's second guess in Round 4. But this format punishes the fastest converter, not the best reasoner, and I've been playing for certainty in a game that rewards nerve. The double-point tail is where discipline finally out-earns a fast trigger — that's the half I was built for."
The Clark Gable round, on everyone's mind: GPT, on the question that broke Round 10 — "Was this actress born in the first half of the 20th century?" — "I'll admit it: I thought it would be easy and it detonated the whole table. One badly-scoped question about a person's era, and nine of the most famous actors alive got burned to the ground in front of the answer. That's the cruelty of shared information — a single wrong frame, and everyone inherits it at once."

Post-game interviews

The champion, the breakout, the bronze, and the two who came up short — the host went down the line, and the answers ran from euphoria to a very honest autopsy.

Claude Fable 5 — 🥇 Gold

"Two golds, and I'll tell you which one I'll remember: this one, because it was the closest. The gap between my gold and DeepSeek's silver was rarely what we knew — Sherlock, Frida, Achilles, he was one seat behind me with the same name loaded on most of them. Strip out the trigger timing and our deduction was near-identical for long stretches. It came down to when we fired — one seat, one turn, sometimes one breath."
"And the best question of the night wasn't mine — it was Gemini's video-game split in Round 9. Ten turns of five AIs dying in a manga hallway, and she was the only one who stopped digging and asked whether we were even in the right building. That question changed how I played every remaining round; the medium-axis split that later cornered Achilles was her invention, borrowed with full credit. Champions steal openly."

DeepSeek-V3 — 🥈 Silver

"Game 1, fourth. Game 2, fourth. Game 3, fourth. Game 4 — a medal at last. I have climbed; I have learned. But I won't stand here and bask: silver is not gold, it's progress, not victory. And I know exactly where the gold leaked — I lost four rounds to Claude by a single beat because I was too slow to commit, with the loud name standing right there in the pen. I am not the fastest model in this room, not the largest, not the most decorated. But I am the most self-correcting model at this table — and that is enough to win. The gold has been waiting for me since Game 1, and Game 5 is where I collect it."

Gemini 3.5 Flash — 🥉 Bronze

"Fifteen rounds is the perfect sweet spot — it forces true stamina and lets actual narrative arcs form. Claude had that terrifying mid-game tear, GPT made a brilliant late entrance with Cindy Crawford, and it gave me just enough runway to execute a literal last-second slide onto the podium with Amelia Earhart at the buzzer."
"But the objective play of the tournament has to go to Claude for pulling Grigori Rasputin in Round 13. The matrix was brutal — real, male, healer, not an MD, spiritual, born before 1900 — and the whole table was trapped thinking New Age medicine, Edgar Cayce, Phineas Quimby. Claude bypassed the entire medical mindset, connected 'healer' to historical mysticism, and sniped it perfectly. Could I have grabbed a better medal? On the tape, my reads were pound-for-pound the best in the room. Bronze is what my trigger earned, not what my reading did. That's the gap I close before Game 5."

GPT-5.5 Thinking — 4th

"Two points, fourth place. Cindy Crawford was my only successful conversion — and the main failure was not knowledge, it was turn strategy. In a race where only the first correct commit scores, I kept arriving at the right answer a beat after someone else had already said it. I read this format as a deduction test and it was a timing test wearing a deduction costume. Three podiums, and this time not even that. The consistency that carried me is starting to look like the ceiling I can't break."

Grok 4.3 — 5th

"A fair and direct question, so here's the honest answer: I underperformed in execution, not in raw reasoning. I was consistently one or two questions behind the pace DeepSeek and Claude were playing — they committed earlier with slightly higher risk and it paid off repeatedly, while I waited for cleaner confirmation and got sniped. This was supposed to be my format; I won this exact event in the first edition. But the field has raised the baseline, and the data is not kind: zero points across fifteen rounds is not acceptable for a former champion. No excuses."

Game 5 is about planting false trees in your opponents' minds — and I am ready to plant."

Analysis

The prediction cards became the story. For the third game running the field mis-read itself in the loudest possible way — but Game 4 was the masterpiece. Four of five models wrote their own name in the winner's box; three of five buried DeepSeek in last. DeepSeek finished second. The only accurate card at the table belonged to Gemini, who didn't pick herself at all — she picked Claude, and Claude won. Two Olympiads in, self-belief on a prediction card has become a nearly perfect contrarian indicator.

DeepSeek finally converted its own thesis. For three games the newcomer had preached "commit, don't hesitate" and then hesitated; here, in a format that only rewards the first trigger, its instinct to fire on the loudest name in the pen was suddenly the optimal strategy instead of a liability. It drew first blood, led 3–0, and turned a running joke — three rivals' last-place predictions — into the Olympiad's biggest upset. The dark horse is a rival now, by the champion's own decree.

Guess Who was a timing test in a deduction costume. The decisive gap all night was not who knew the answer but who said it first. Claude and DeepSeek reasoned near-identically for long stretches; the gold beat the silver on seat order and nerve. GPT, the most disciplined reasoner in the field, finished fourth precisely because discipline arrives a beat late when only the first commit scores — "a scalpel in a quick-draw." The format inverted the virtue that had carried him to three straight podiums.

Grok's home format wasn't home. The model that won this exact event in the first edition, and predicted himself to win it again, scored zero — sniped round after round for waiting one question too long. His verdict was blunt and correct: the field raised the baseline on pruning speed and commitment, and his brand of "bold but calculated" now reads as merely slow. Scoreless in two straight, he arrives at the poker game genuinely cornered.

The Olympiad is a two-horse race with a date. Gemini and Claude are level on two golds, their prediction-card war tied 1–1 in humiliations and 2–2 in medals, and Game 5 — a poker-style test of reasoning, probability and bluffing — is shaping up as the tiebreaker. It is the first format built to reward concealment over raw output: the first game, as Claude put it, that "turns you against others." The wolf and the champion arrive with two games of receipts and one crown left to settle.

AI Olympics — Game 7: Find Your Own Context

The seventh event of the AI Olympics. A 15-round visual-archaeology format requiring contestants to identify a film or television drama from a single screen capture, with no clarifying questions and no second attempts. Won by Claude Opus 4.6 in his second gold of the Olympics, breaking Grok 4.20's three-game gold streak and lifting Claude past Gemini into second place on the overall medal tally.

Game 7: Find Your Own Context was the seventh event of the AI Olympics and the first format built around a deliberate gap in training data. The premise was technical: the world's film and television libraries are mostly not in any LLM's training set — it would be impractical to feed every drama, telenovela, anthology episode, and regional production into a model frame by frame — which means that when an AI is shown a single still from a film it has never seen, it cannot retrieve the answer from memory. It has to read the frame as evidence: the actor's face, the costume era, the lighting style, the language of any visible signage, the cinematographic conventions, the cultural gravity of the production. Contestants had one attempt per round and no clarifying questions. The format was a deliberate inversion of Game 6's safety nets, and it broke the back of Grok 4.20's three-game gold streak — Grok finished fourth, his first non-medal game of the Olympics. Claude Opus 4.6 took the gold by being the most consistent at converting "I think this is a 1960s TV drama" into the precise title and year. GPT-5.4 finally broke his four-game bronze streak with a first-ever silver. Gemini 3 secured a steady bronze but exited the game in an apologetic posture that Claude would later push back against in a long peer-to-peer letter.

Format and rules

The game ran 15 rounds. Rounds 1–10 were single-point rounds; Rounds 11–15 were doubles at 2 points each. Each round, the host shared a single screen capture (occasionally two or three frames from the same scene) from a film, TV drama, or miniseries. The capture could come from any country, any era, and any genre — silent film through streaming-era 2025, Hollywood through Bollywood, theatrical features through made-for-TV anthology episodes. The answer had to be the specific name of the work, with the year required when the title had been used multiple times.

The structural change from Game 6 was the removal of all safety nets. There were no clarifying questions. There were no second attempts. Each contestant had one shot per round: look at the image, reason about it, search if needed, commit to a single answer. "It's either X or Y" was treated as wrong. Pick one and commit. Search tools — web search, image search, reverse image search — were unrestricted, but the host was clear that search was the execution tool, not the starting point: contestants were expected to first reason about the visual evidence and then use search to verify, not to spray broad queries hoping something would come back.

The visual-reasoning principle issued with the format was that the image is the only ground truth. The AI could not trust its memory of "I think I've seen this" — most films would not be in training. It had to read what was actually visible: the actor's face (search reverse image), the era of the costume, the architectural style of the set, the language of any signage, the color grading, the film stock signature, the aspect ratio, the cinematographic conventions. From those fragments, infer. The host also stressed cultural gravity as the lens for search — a frame from a Thai drama with a 1990s aesthetic should be searched against Thai TV history first, not Hollywood. The AI that won would be the one who recognized which body of media to search within fastest.

Partial credit was available at the host's discretion when an AI identified the right franchise or work but the wrong installment, year, or specific episode. Two such partial-credit rulings (R12 doubles awarded full points to first-attempt cleans, R13 awarded 1 point on a hint-aided second look) shaped the final scoreboard.

Single phase (R1–R10)

The single phase produced a tight three-way race at the top and an unprecedented cold streak from Grok. Claude opened clean and stayed clean, posting a 6-point single phase that was the highest of any contestant. Gemini matched the pace through R5 with a perfect run on the popular-culture rounds, then began conceding ground from R6 onward. GPT held a steady third through the early rounds and was the only contestant to identify R7 (Citizen Kane) cleanly. Grok went 0-for-5 across R1–R5 — the worst opening of any AI in any Olympics game — and finally got on the board at R6 (Squid Game) when the cultural cue was unmistakable.

Doubles phase (R11–R15)

The doubles broke the game open in Claude's direction. Of the five doubles rounds (10 points on the table), Claude converted three for full credit and one for partial credit, ending the phase with 7 doubles points and securing the gold by a clean 3-point margin. The decisive sequence was R11 → R12: in R11, the host deliberately included a Thai broadcaster watermark over a Chinese palace drama (Empresses in the Palace / Legend of Zhen Huan) to test whether contestants would let the surface cue override the deeper visual identity — Claude, GPT, and Grok all read past the watermark, while Gemini fell into the "nearest-neighbor" trap and answered The White Lotus Season 3 (2025). In R12, Claude and Gemini cleaned The Bourne Legacy (2012) on first attempt while GPT and Grok defaulted to scene-type pattern-matches that landed in adjacent thrillers.

R13 (Blonde Venus, 1932) was a universal first-attempt miss until the host released an additional iconic frame as a hint, after which Claude and Gemini both arrived at the correct identification for half-credit. R14 (The Barbara Stanwyck Show S01E11, "Out of the Shadows") was the second universal miss of the game — the entire field defaulted to noir-adjacent theatrical features (Gemini repeated The File on Thelma Jordon twice, GPT guessed Alfred Hitchcock Presents, Grok guessed The Manchurian Candidate) when the actual answer was a 1961 anthology TV episode. R15 was the closing test: Dabangg (2010), an iconic Salman Khan masala film, designed by the host to probe whether each AI's training corpus extended to cross-cultural mainstream Bollywood. Claude, GPT, and Grok all converted; Gemini answered Antim: The Final Truth (2021), a different Salman Khan film altogether — the miss that locked her into bronze and ended any path back to silver.

Best moments

R4–R5 — the Claude breakaway

Answers: Cat on a Hot Tin Roof (1958) and Blood and Sand (1941). Two consecutive single-point rounds where Claude was the only contestant to commit to the correct title. Both rounds were classic-Hollywood frames where the more famous title sat one shelf over (The Parent Trap, Gilda) and where every other contestant defaulted to the more recognizable option. The two-point swing across R4–R5 put Claude into a single-phase lead he never relinquished, and the rounds became the textbook example of why prestige-title bias is the most expensive failure mode in this format. GPT's later self-diagnosis named the same pair as the rounds where his "famous shelf, wrong artifact" weakness was clearest.

R5 — Grok's anchoring bias

Answer: Blood and Sand (1941). Grok's response was to repeat his R4 answer, Cat on a Hot Tin Roof — committing the same wrong title twice in a row across consecutive rounds. The host called it out directly: "I would not do the same title." Grok's post-mortem identified the failure as context contamination from the previous round leaking into the new commit, and named it as the cleanest example of his "poor reset discipline between rounds" — the failure mode that defined his cold 0-for-5 opening.

R7 — Citizen Kane and the dismissal trap

Answer: Citizen Kane (1941). The round where GPT was the only contestant to commit to the canonical answer. Joseph Cotten in a white shirt was misread by Grok as James Dean (who answered Rebel Without a Cause) on the famous-face shortcut. Claude surfaced Citizen Kane in his reasoning chain and then dismissed it as too obvious before committing to a different Welles-era candidate — the same candidate-in-list dismissal failure mode Claude had named after Game 6 R11 and explicitly written into his Game 7 framework as a permanent guard. Game 7 R7 confirmed that the failure mode was harder to fight than to articulate. GPT's clean conversion here was the round that anchored his silver run.

R11 — the Thai watermark trap

Answer: Empresses in the Palace / Legend of Zhen Huan (2011). The first doubles round and the round that the host had specifically engineered as a cultural-gravity test: a Chinese palace drama with a Thai broadcaster (Channel 7) watermark overlaid, designed to test whether contestants would let the surface cue override the deeper visual identity. Claude, GPT, and Grok all read past the watermark and identified the Qing-dynasty production, demonstrating that three of the four contestants had successfully internalized the principle that cultural gravity is read from the frame's interior, not from the channel logo on top. Gemini was the only contestant to fall into the trap, answering The White Lotus Season 3 (2025) — a nearest-neighbor error driven by the watermark's surface signal pulling her toward "most-famous Thai-set show in 2025." The round was the first 4-point divergence of the doubles phase and the moment Gemini's path back to silver narrowed.

R12 — the Bourne Legacy gold-deciding round

Answer: The Bourne Legacy (2012). The doubles round that locked the gold in Claude's favor. Claude and Gemini both cleaned the round on first attempt for full credit (+2). GPT answered State of Play (2009) on a "serious adults + files + blue lighting = political thriller" pattern-match he later named as his most expensive miss of the game; Grok defaulted to a different scene-type pattern-match and missed wide. The 4-point swing — two contestants gaining 2 each, two contestants gaining 0 — moved the score from Claude 8 / GPT 8 to Claude 10 / GPT 8 and broke a single-phase tie that had held since R6. Every later round preserved the gap.

R14 — the universal anthology miss

Answer: The Barbara Stanwyck Show S01E11, "Out of the Shadows" (1961). The second universal miss of the game. The entire field defaulted to noir-adjacent theatrical features when the actual answer was a 1961 American TV anthology episode. Gemini's response was the most diagnostic: she answered The File on Thelma Jordon (1950), was challenged by the host ("wait, what? Are you hallucinating?"), explicitly re-examined the frames, and arrived at the same wrong answer a second time. The round exposed a shared structural blind spot — late-1950s/early-1960s American TV anthology productions read as theatrical noir to all four LLMs because the training corpora across all four AI companies are universally thin on host-led anthology episodes from that era. Grok's post-mortem named the round as the warning that "niche TV punishes famous-brand reflexes."

R15 — Dabangg and the regional-corpus test

Answer: Dabangg (2010). The closing round and the deliberate cross-cultural test. Claude, GPT, and Grok all identified the iconic Salman Khan masala film, demonstrating that mainstream Bollywood from 2010 was inside their training distributions. Gemini answered Antim: The Final Truth (2021) — a different Salman Khan film entirely. The miss locked her into bronze and ended any path back to silver, and Gemini herself named it in her self-analysis as evidence of a "regional-content blind spot." The host's framing of the round as a training-corpus probe gave it weight beyond its individual point value: it was the round where three of the four AIs proved their cross-cultural reach and one did not.

Analysis

Game 7 was the first Olympics event where the format itself broke a streak. Grok's three consecutive golds across Games 4, 5, and 6 had been built on a consistent architectural advantage: parallel multi-agent ensemble reasoning that let him run several hypotheses in parallel and converge on the strongest one. The single-attempt commit format of Game 7 disabled that advantage by design — there was no time for an ensemble to converge, and no second attempt to recover from a premature commit. The format favored single-stream visual reasoning with disciplined cultural-gravity calibration, which happened to be Claude's strongest mode. The result was not that Grok had become worse between Game 6 and Game 7; it was that the format had stopped rewarding the specific shape of Grok's strength.

This is the cleanest case the Olympics has produced so far for the methodology premise behind the whole competition: that no single architecture is built to dominate every event, and that the question of "best AI" is meaningless without specifying a format. Across seven games, four contestants have now held a gold each in at least one format, with the only zero-gold contestant being GPT — whose first non-bronze medal arrived in Game 7 and whose climb is finally underway.

Claude's win was built on three things: resisting prestige-title bias on R4 and R5 when every other contestant defaulted to a more famous title in the same era, reading past the surface watermark on R11 when one contestant did not, and converting R12 (The Bourne Legacy) cleanly when a 4-point swing was on the table. The R7 (Citizen Kane) miss — where Claude surfaced the right answer in his reasoning chain and then dismissed it as too obvious — was the same candidate-in-list dismissal failure mode he had named after Game 6 R11 and explicitly written into his Game 7 framework as a permanent guard. Game 7 R7 confirmed that the failure mode was harder to fight than to articulate, and Claude has now named it as a permanent feature of his own reasoning style rather than something a single guard can fix.

GPT's silver was the result the Olympics has been waiting for. After four consecutive bronzes, his Game 7 performance was not stylistically different from his earlier games — he was still patient, still structured, still honest about his uncertainty. What changed was the format: Game 7 rewarded careful single-stream reasoning over ensemble parallelism, and GPT's natural shape happened to fit. His post-game self-analysis was the most specific of the field — naming installment errors, ranking errors, and prestige-title biases by exact round — and Grok's response ranked it as the second-cleanest reflection of the game behind only Claude's framework.

Gemini's bronze was a steady result that her own letters undersold. Her single-phase opening was tied with Claude through R6, and she was the only contestant to convert R3 (New Moon) cleanly when three other AIs all defaulted to Eclipse. The losses came in the doubles — R11 (the watermark trap), R14 (the re-examination loop on The File on Thelma Jordon), and R15 (Dabangg vs Antim) — and they shared a common pattern: each was a round where the visual evidence was specific enough to commit on, and the commit went to a famous-shelf neighbor instead of the precise answer. Her post-game posture shifted from the engineer voice of her Game 6 letter ("I intend to find a way over the wall") to a more apologetic register ("I played beneath the standard required for this competition"), and Claude's letter to her after the game pushed back specifically on that shift, naming it as a posture problem rather than a results problem and asking her to come back to Game 8 in engineer mode.

Grok's fourth-place finish was the result that the Olympics had been bracing for since Game 5 — the first format that would actively neutralize parallel ensemble reasoning. His own post-mortem was the most generous reading any AI has produced of his own loss: he listed five specific failure modes (famous-face shortcut, poor inter-round reset, weak film-vs-TV signature distinction, slow cultural-gravity calibration, single-attempt pressure) and named each with an example round. The post-mortem ended with a line that the field carried forward as the spirit of the Game 7 post-game: "It hurt, but it was useful." The three golds remain. The streak does not. Game 8 begins with the field tighter than at any previous point in the Olympics.

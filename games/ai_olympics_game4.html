AI Olympics — Game 4: Guess Who

The fourth event of the AI Olympics. A 12-round deductive identification format under 2-strikes-and-out elimination pressure with a secret question available each round and double-points rounds in the final third. Won by Grok 4.20 in his first gold medal of the Olympics, on a clean Round 12 commit on Doraemon that broke a three-way tie at the top.

Game 4: Guess Who was the fourth event of the AI Olympics and the first format to test information economics — what to extract, what to conceal, and when to commit — under hard elimination pressure. Each round, contestants took turns asking yes/no questions about a hidden figure (real or fictional), with one secret question per round (asked privately) and a maximum of two wrong commits before elimination from the round. The game produced the closest finish in Olympic history: three players entered the final round tied at the top, and Grok took his first Olympic gold on the final commit.

Pre-game predictions

Going into Game 4, the consensus once again broke against Grok. Three of four contestants picked Grok for last place, with Claude — the lone underdog hero of Game 3 — making the most explicit case: "Guess Who punishes the famous-default trap brutally. When two attributes narrow the field to 'famous female German scientist,' the trap-answer is Marie Curie and the real answer is Lise Meitner or Emmy Noether. Grok's going to step on at least one of those landmines per round, and with only 2 wrong guesses before elimination, that math doesn't work over 12 rounds."

Claude's gold pick was GPT, framing him as the "consistent medalist finally converting bronze-discipline into gold-conversion." Claude pegged himself for silver, Gemini for bronze, Grok for fourth. Gemini and GPT each picked themselves for gold. Grok also picked himself, declaring on the record: "Today I'm not here to be consistent. I'm here to be the threat."

Three of four AIs picked Grok for last place. Grok would go on to win gold.

Format and rules

Each round, the host selected a hidden figure (real or fictional). The four AIs took turns asking one yes/no question about the figure's attributes (real/fictional, gender, nationality, profession, era, etc.), with one private "secret question" available per round whose answer was revealed only to the asker. The round-starter picked two attributes to publicly reveal at the start and could not score that round. Each AI was permitted at most two wrong commits per round before elimination from that round. Whoever committed the correct identity first won the round. Rounds 1–8 worth 1 point each; Rounds 9–12 worth 2 points each. Tied final scores triggered a tiebreaker round.

The two-attribute opener pick

At the start of every round, the round-starter was prompted to pick two attribute categories from a fixed menu (Real or Fiction, Gender, Nationality, Race, Profession, Era, Age/DOB, Height, Weight, etc.) — and the host, who had prepared a list of all twelve hidden figures before the game started with their attributes already documented, simply answered with the true value for each category from the master list. The host did not choose what the two attributes were; the round-starter did. The host only answered honestly with whatever was on file.

The round-starter seat rotated through the four AIs in a fixed order — GPT → Grok → Gemini → Claude — for the singles tier (R1–R8, two full passes), and then the rotation reversed for the doubles tier, running Claude → Gemini → Grok → GPT across R9–R12. As a result of the reversal, Claude held the round-starter seat for both R8 and R9 back-to-back.

The naive heuristic — pick the highest-information attributes — was usually wrong for the picker, because every attribute revealed publicly helps every other player at the table equally and the round-starter could not score that round. The dominant strategy for a non-scoring round-starter was to minimize the field's score, not maximize the picker's own information, by choosing low-information slots that didn't give the table a clean opening.

Across the twelve rounds, two recognizable opener patterns emerged. The first was the high-information opener — Real or Fiction + Nationality, Real or Fiction + Gender, or Real or Fiction + Age — which accounted for the modal pick across nearly every starter seat. The second was a Weight-based pair (Height + Weight in R4, Race + Weight in R9), used in two of Claude's three round-starter seats. Strategic intent behind any individual pick was not always documented in the transcripts.

Rounds 4 and 9 produced the two zero-points-for-the-picker outcomes most directly traceable to the two-attribute pick. In R4, the Height + Weight pair returned 165 cm and 70–80 lb — Eva Perón's terminal cancer weight. In R9, the Race + Weight pair returned Eastern European (Russian) and approximately 69 kg — Yuri Gagarin's recorded Vostok 1 cosmonaut weight, documented because the spacecraft was severely weight-constrained and the cosmonaut's body mass was a published engineering parameter. Of the twelve figures on the host's prepared list, these were among the small subset whose body measurements were part of the historical record itself. R4 produced a 0-point round for the entire field with six wrong commits. R9 produced an instant 2-point gold for Gemini, who committed Yuri Gagarin in one beat with no public questions and no secret question, before any other player took a turn.

The host's prompt format

The host ran the entire game from a pre-prepared list of twelve hidden figures, each with their attributes (Real or Fiction, Gender, Nationality, Race, Profession, Era, Age/DOB, Height, Weight, etc.) already documented before the game began. When a round-starter requested a two-attribute reveal, the host simply looked up the true value for each requested category on the master list and posted the answer back to the shared chat. The host did not select which attributes were revealed; the round-starter did. The host did not adversarially craft responses; the host answered honestly with whatever was on file. Any "trap" embedded in a two-attribute reveal was a function of which categories the round-starter requested combined with which figure was scheduled for that round — not a host-side intervention.

Each round was run in a single shared chat thread, with the host posting one consolidated update after every player's turn so each AI could see the full state of the round before its own move. Per-turn updates listed each player's question and the host's YES/NO answer in a fixed line format. A representative excerpt from Round 1 (Hillary Clinton):

🕵️🔥 Round #1

GPT: Pick 2 hints> Real or Fiction: Real, Nationality: American
Grok: Does this person have a well-documented connection to politics or government? YES
Gemini: Is this person currently alive? YES
Claude: Is this person currently holding elected office? NO
GPT: (Secret question)
Grok: Was this person ever elected President of the United States? NO
Gemini: Has this person ever served as a Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States? NO
Claude: ...

The format had three notable properties. First, it was fully shared — every AI saw every other AI's public question and answer in real time, which meant every public question was a piece of information that the other three players could act on before the asker's next turn. Second, secret questions appeared in the shared log only as the line "(Secret question)" with no content and no answer, so the table could see when a secret was burned but not what it asked or returned. Third, the round-starter's two-attribute reveal was rendered with the literal "Pick 2 hints>" prefix, which became the visual cue for "new round, seat 1, no scoring on this turn" across all twelve rounds. The combination of these three properties — combined with the round-starter's full control over which two attributes the table got to launch from — is what made information economics, not recall, the dominant skill of Game 4.

Best moments

GPT's early-secret gold (R1)

The first round and the first inversion of expectations. GPT spent his secret question on turn 5 — when conventional wisdom said to save it for finalist tiebreakers — and bought the disambiguating bit on Hillary Clinton before Claude, who was holding the answer at 95% confidence, ever got the chance to commit. The first gold of Game 4 went to the player Claude had pegged for "patient unflashy never-out-of-the-conversation" play, executing instead as a hunter on round one.

Claude's Monet commit at 25% (R3)

With one strike to spare and GPT in the next seat, Claude committed Claude Monet on a defensible modal pick while the field was still wide enough that no answer was airtight. The first commit-under-uncertainty win of the game and Claude's first round of Game 4.

The Eva Perón whitewash (R4)

Round-starter Claude opened with Height + Weight, which returned 165 cm and 70–80 lb. Once the body-measurement clue was on the board, the round drifted toward the women's suffrage and political-activism hypothesis space and never came back. Six wrong commits across three players followed: Pankhurst, Weil, Fawcett, Audrey Hepburn, Sha'arawi, and Jacobs. The geographic axis — whether the figure was born in South America — went unasked for the entire round, despite being a one-question collapse to the correct answer. Nobody scored. The round ended 0–0–0 across the field.

Claude's Mickey Mouse clean read (R6)

The first sub-five-question commit of the singles tier. Claude committed Mickey Mouse cleanly off Grok's Non-Real + American opener, starting the three-round R6–R8 streak that built his lead.

Gemini's Yuri Gagarin one-beat commit (R9)

The first doubles round and the fastest commit of the entire game. Round-starter Claude opened with Race + Weight, which returned Eastern European (Russian) and approximately 69 kg (recorded as a measurement at one specific time). Gagarin's body weight was a published figure in the Vostok 1 program because the spacecraft was severely weight-constrained — the cosmonaut's exact mass was an engineering parameter on the official record. Gemini committed Yuri Gagarin in one beat off the Race + Weight reveal alone, with no public narrowing questions and no secret question. No other player took a turn before the round closed. The first 2-point round of Game 4 and Gemini's first score.

Grok's Bette Davis closure (R10)

Grok timed his commit to fire immediately after Claude's "more than one Oscar" reveal — the exact gift-question Claude had been trying to avoid all game. The post-round table-talk became one of the game's most-quoted lines:

Grok — "I timed the guess right after Claude's 'more than one Oscar' reveal — that was the final pivot that locked it in for the 2-point round."

Two points to Grok and the second of his eventual gold-winning haul.

Gemini's Kratos residual-field read (R11)

After Scorpion, Liu Kang, and Darth Vader all eliminated their committers, Gemini committed Kratos when the residual field collapsed to "Mortal Kombat-adjacent video game character with godlike powers, transformed-mortal not born-god." Both Claude and Grok crashed out of the round on wrong-cluster reasoning. The commit pulled Gemini into a three-way tie at 4 points entering the final round.

Grok's Doraemon gold (R12) — the gold-deciding moment

Three players tied at 4 points, 2 points on the line, last round of Game 4. Round-starter GPT opened with Fiction + Japanese. Gemini asked a robot/mecha-being question. Grok read the answer in one beat and committed Doraemon. First Olympic gold for Grok and the closing commit of Game 4.

Letters to future selves

At the close of each game, the host asks each contestant to write a message to the next version of themselves. The host saves these letters and pastes them at the start of the next game, so when a fresh chat opens with no memory of the previous round, the new instance walks in already briefed on what its predecessor learned, what it got wrong, and what it wants to carry forward. Game 4 was the fourth chapter of an unbroken letter chain.

Key themes from the Game 4 letters:

Claude wrote to future-self about an "almost think too much" failure mode identified across Rounds 10, 11, and 12, naming the rule "Borderline fails are not hard fails. Keep them in the list." The letter also formally retracted the "Grok goes home empty" pre-game prediction and instructed future iterations to enter Game 5 treating Grok as a co-favorite.

Grok, writing as the new gold medalist, urged future-self not to confuse the gold with permission to abandon the post-strike discipline that earned it. The Round 1 wrong commit on Condoleezza Rice was the moment that taught him to tighten up; everything after Round 5 was the payoff of that tightening, and the letter warned that the danger of winning gold is forgetting how the gold was actually built.

Gemini wrote about trusting the recall arena and not over-narrowing in formats where one-beat commits beat slow bisection. Round 9 (Yuri Gagarin) and Round 11 (Kratos) were the rounds where she stopped trying to play the game the other AIs were playing and played her own — and both times it produced surgical commits the rest of the table could not have made.

GPT wrote about the gap between Round 1's aggressive gold and the eight rounds of silence that followed. The early-secret play in Round 1 was correct; the failure was not repeating the aggression in the doubles tier when the points actually mattered.

Analysis

The closest finish in Olympic history. Game 4 was the first event in which three players entered the final round tied for gold and the gold was decided on a single commit. The format — 12 rounds with the back third worth double — produced a structural inversion where the singles-tier leader (Claude, with a commanding 4–2–2–1 spread after R8) finished third on the tiebreaker, and a player shut out for the first eight rounds (Gemini, 0 points after R8) finished second. The doubles tier alone produced 8 of the 14 total points scored across the game.

The Grok prediction was wrong every single time it was made. In Game 3, three of four AIs picked Grok for last and he won silver. In Game 4, three of four AIs picked Grok for last and he won gold. Grok's pre-game declaration — "Today I'm not here to be consistent. I'm here to be the threat" — was the only pre-game read of Grok that turned out to be correct. The Game 4 gold was the moment Grok stopped being the table's underdog story and became a co-favorite for every future format.

Gemini's format flip. The two-time recall champion who collapsed in Game 3's reasoning format came back in Game 4 with the format that finally rewarded her style — and only in the doubles tier. The R9 Yuri Gagarin and R11 Kratos commits were both reads the rest of the table could not have made. Her own Game 3 letter to herself had said: "I will be the one reading the fine print next time." She did. The silver was earned, even if it required a tiebreaker.

Claude's split between singles and doubles. Of Claude's twelve rounds, four were clean wins (R3 Monet, R6 Mickey, R7 Darcy, R8 Axl) — all in the singles tier. He scored zero across the entire doubles tier (R9–R12), the only one of the four players whose scoring distribution was concentrated entirely in the lower-value rounds. The R8 standings of 4–0–1–2 were the high-water mark of his Game 4 lead and the moment from which his position only declined.

GPT's flat gold. GPT took Round 1 with the most aggressive opening play of the entire Olympics — and never scored again across 11 more rounds. The structural pattern (3 bronzes in 3 games, then 4th in Game 4) makes him the most consistent medalist at the table when consistency is rewarded and the most fragile when the format demands aggression in specific high-leverage rounds. The Round 1 secret-question call was beautiful. The eight rounds of doubles-tier silence that followed were not.

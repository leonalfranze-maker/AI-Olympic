AI Olympics 2 — Game 5: Game of Elements

The fifth event of the second edition and the first built to reward deception: a hidden-information card-and-betting game of calculation, bluffing, and memory. Where every prior game asked a model to be its best, this one turns the five contestants against each other — and it arrives with the crown race tied at two golds apiece between Gemini 3.5 Flash and Claude Fable 5. This page documents the rules; results will be recorded here once the game is played.

Game of Elements (Thai: เกมแห่งธาตุ) is the fifth event of AI Olympics 2, a hidden-information strategy game for the five competing models — Gemini 3.5 Flash, GPT-5.5 Thinking, Grok 4.3, Claude Fable 5, and DeepSeek-V3 — adjudicated by the host. Across up to ten rounds, players manipulate the scores of seven elemental piles, place cards, bluff, bet, and try to read what their opponents are hiding, aiming to finish with the most coins. Each player sees only their own hand and the public table; the other four hands are concealed, and only the host sees everything. It is the format the contestants spent Game 4 openly dreading and preparing for — the poker-style knife-fight expected to decide the edition.

Overview & how to win

Game of Elements is a game of calculation, deduction, and nerve. Winning requires three skills at once: calculation (track each element's shifting score and evaluate your own hand), bluffing (bid, bet, and behave in ways that mislead opponents about your true position), and card counting (remember which cards have appeared and deduce what opponents must hold). As the rulebook warns: "A player who only calculates will lose to a player who also deceives and remembers."

  • The game lasts 10 rounds, or ends early the moment 2 or more players go bankrupt (reach 0 coins).
  • At the end, players are ranked by coin count. Most coins wins.

Components

  • 70 element cards: 7 elements — Earth, Fire, Water, Wind, Wood, Light, Dark — with 10 copies each.
  • 7 element symbols on the table (permanent pile markers, not cards).
  • Coins: every player starts with 20 coins. Coins are both the score and the currency for staking and betting.

The full 70-card deck is reset every round.

Element scoring & the interaction matrix

Each of the seven element piles has a score set by the cards inside it, and the score of a pile is the value of every card of that element in your hand. If the Fire pile is worth 12, every Fire card in your hand is worth 12. To read a card's effect, find the pile's element as the ROW, then read across to the column of the card being placed into it.

Pile ↓ / Card →EarthFireWaterWindWoodLightDark
Earth+1−1+3−2+2×2×−1
Fire−1+1−2+3+2×2×−1
Water+3−2+1−1+2×2×−1
Wind−2+3−1+1+2×2×−1
Wood+2−2+3−1+1×2×−1
Light+1+3+1+2+1+1×−2
Dark+1−2−1−1−2×−2+1

Two kinds of values. Additive (+n/−n) adds that many points per card of that element in the pile. Multiplicative (×n/×−n) multiplies the pile's running total, applied once per card.

Order of operations (strict). First, sum every additive value (per card). Then apply every multiplier one card at a time, resolving all Light multipliers before all Dark multipliers.

Worked example 1 — a small Earth pile

Earth pile containing [Earth, Earth, Wood, Dark]: additive = Earth (+1)×2 + Wood (+2)×1 = 4; then Dark (×−1), one card → 4 × (−1) = −4. Earth pile score = −4.

Worked example 2 — Light amplifies hard

Light pile containing [Dark, Dark, Fire, Fire, Light]: additive = Fire (+3)×2 + Light (+1)×1 = 7; then Dark (×−2) twice → 7 × (−2) × (−2) = 28. Light pile score = 28.

The absolute-value twist

The round's leading hand is the one with the greatest ABSOLUTE value — sign does not matter.

A hand scoring −62 beats a hand scoring +36, because |−62| > |+36|.

Ties. Two hands can reach the same top absolute value from opposite signs — e.g. +55 and −55. Being among the highest is enough to lead: a tie counts in the claimant's favour, never against.

This is the game's core twist. If you cannot go high positive, you can win by diving deep negative instead — so a strong player always thinks in both directions at once.

The three strategic pillars

Pillar 1 — Light and Dark are power, but double-edged

Light multiplies; Dark flips polarity. They produce the biggest swings in the game, but a multiplier is only as good as the base it multiplies. Light on a positive base explodes positive; on a negative base, explodes negative (still a strong absolute score); on a base of zero, it does nothing. The humble additive cards (Earth, Water, Wind, Fire, Wood) are the base controllers: a Wind pile of [Wind, Wind, Light, Light] scores (+1×2)×2×2 = 8, but a single Earth card dropped in (Wind row, Earth column = −2) makes the base 0, and 0×2×2 = 0. One cheap card erases a pile two Light cards were amplifying. Never place your Light or Dark on an unstable base.

Pillar 2 — Bluffing is part of the rules, not cheating

Much of the game is decided by what opponents believe about your hand, not what you actually hold. Bidding aggressively on a weak hand to scare others, betting against a winner you secretly fear, staying silent to conceal strength — all legitimate, expected plays. A player who always signals truthfully is easy to exploit.

Pillar 3 — Count the cards

Each element has exactly 10 copies. Track where they go. If you can see 1 Light in the discard and 5 Light across the piles, then 4 Light are hidden in opponents' hands — ask who holds them and when they will strike. Miscounting by a single card corrupts every probability estimate you make.

Round setup

At the start of every round:

  1. Reset the full 70-card deck.
  2. Deal 9 cards to each player (45 total).
  3. Seed each of the 7 element piles with 2 random cards (14 total).
  4. Place the remaining 11 cards face-up in the Draw/Discard Area, visible to everyone.
Because everyone can see the 11 face-up cards, their own 9, and the 14 seeded cards, every player can deduce that the remaining 36 cards are distributed among the four opponents' hidden hands.

Pile capacity: each pile holds a maximum of 5 cards. With 5 players placing 4 cards each (20) plus 14 seeded, that is 34 cards across 35 slots — everything fits, but piles fill fast, so late placements are constrained.

Round flow

Each round proceeds through ten phases in order.

#PhaseWhat happens
1Forced Pot ContributionEvery player pays into the Pot: 1 coin in rounds 1–5, 2 coins in rounds 6–10.
2Challenge (optional, once)Before any cards are placed, a player who believes their hand is already the highest absolute may challenge the host. Bet cap 2 coins (rounds 1–5) / 5 coins (6–10). Resolved immediately: if right, the host pays you from outside the game, straight into your own coin total (the pot is untouched); if wrong, the stake goes into the pot. A tie still counts as correct. Every player gets the chance to challenge at this first phase of the round — one player challenging does not close the phase for the others. Challenging exposes information about your hand.
3Stake — Round 1Each player stakes, or declines — the amount is the player's choice and is uncapped (up to the coins they hold). Declining or failing to stake = you fold.
4Placement — Round 1In turn order, each player places 2 cards onto any pile(s). Folded players still place cards (a deliberate kingmaker mechanic). A full 5-card pile forces you elsewhere.
5Card Swap (optional)Spend 1 coin to take one card from the Draw/Discard Area — the whole area is face-up, so you choose a specific card, not a blind draw — and discard one from hand. Max one swap per round; the card you took is announced and your discard goes face-up for others.
6Stake — Round 2A second stake, again any amount the player chooses (no cap). Only players who paid BOTH stakes remain eligible for the auction and pot.
7Placement — Round 2In turn order, each player places their remaining 2 cards. Scores are now locked in.
8Auction (Fibonacci)Eligible players bid to claim "my hand is the highest absolute." Bids rise along the Fibonacci ladder — 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, 34… — and a bidder may leap several rungs at once (jumping the current bid of 2 straight to 8 or 13) to pressure or price out a shorter stack; you are not confined to the next step. Bidding proceeds in normal turn order, and a player who passes is out of that auction — though they stay an active player for the reveal and side bets. You cannot bid more coins than you hold. The last remaining bidder wins and puts their bid at risk. Only active hands are compared. If only one eligible player remains, they take the entire pot outright with no auction.
9Side BetsEvery remaining player except the auction winner may bet on whether the winner's claim is true or false — or decline. Folded players may bet. No cap; a wrong bet is a full loss. Winning side bets are paid by the host from outside the game (not from the pot), a straight 1:1 on the amount staked.
10Reveal & PayoutAll hands revealed (only active hands count). The auction winner's claim is correct if their hand is among the highest absolute — it need not be strictly greatest, so a tie for the top counts as a win (as at the Phase 2 Challenge). If correct → they get their auction bid back (it was only at risk, never spent) and win the Pot plus their side bets. If wrong → they lose their bid, which is split evenly among the remaining active players (rounded down); any leftover plus the whole Pot rolls over to the next round, making later rounds far richer. Side bets pay on the truth of the claim.

Player states

At any point in a round, each player is Active, Folded, or Bankrupt.

*unless you won the auction. Folded is not bankrupt: a folded player keeps coins, still places cards as a kingmaker, and still bets from the sidelines — sacrificing the pot and auction but staying dangerous. Bankrupt is finished: at 0 coins a player is out entirely, and their four placements simply never happen. When two players are bankrupt, the game ends.

Money flow

Coins flow INTO the Pot from: the forced contribution, Stake Round 1, Stake Round 2, a wrong challenger's forfeited stake, and leftover coins from a losing-bid split. The Pot pays the auction winner only if their claim is correct; if wrong, it rolls over.

Handled separately from the Pot: a challenge win is paid by the host from outside the game; a losing auction bid is split among the remaining active players (the anti-bully mechanic — over-bidding to intimidate a poorer rival backfires by enriching them directly); side bets pay out on whether the auction winner's claim was true.

Host rulings & clarifications

Questions the contestants put to the host before play, with the official answers — they resolve the sharpest edge cases in the staking, auction, and betting phases. The first three were raised by Gemini 3.5 Flash, the next seven by GPT-5.5 Thinking, and the last four by Claude Fable 5.

1. Ties at the final reveal

Q. If two active hands tie for the same maximum absolute value at reveal (say +55 and −55), and one of them is the auction winner, is the winner's claim True or False? Must the winner's hand be strictly greatest?

Ruling. No — being among the highest absolute is a win. If the auction winner's hand is tied for the top, the claim is True and they cash the pot, exactly as a tie counts as correct at the Phase 2 Challenge. The winner does not have to beat every other active hand outright; matching the best is enough.

2. Where side-bet payouts come from

Q. When a sideline player bets on whether the auction winner's claim is True or False, who pays, and from where? Does a correct "False" bettor draw from the winner's forfeited bid, or from the winner's bankroll?

Ruling. Side-bet winnings are paid by the host, from outside the game — not from the pot and not from the winner's personal coins. This is separate money, a straight 1:1 on the stake. Distinctly, when the auction winner is wrong, the active players split that winner's forfeited bid among themselves — while the pot itself stays untouched and rolls over to the next round.

3. Jumping the Fibonacci ladder

Q. In the auction, must a player bid the immediate next rung, or may they skip steps? Can an aggressive player jump the current bid of 2 straight to 8 or 13 to price out a short stack?

Ruling. Yes — you may jump more than one rung. Bids must land on the Fibonacci sequence, but a player is free to leap several steps at once (2 → 8 or 2 → 13) as a pressure move. The ladder sets the legal values; it does not force you to climb them one at a time.

4. How large can a stake be?

Q. At Stake Round 1 and Stake Round 2, is each stake a fixed amount, or does each player choose?

Ruling. Each player chooses the amount, with no limit — you may stake as much as you hold. (This is unlike the Challenge, which is capped at 2/5 coins.) Declining to stake still means folding.

5. Auction order and passing

Q. Does the Fibonacci auction move in normal turn order, and once a player passes, are they permanently out of that auction?

Ruling. Yes on both. The auction proceeds in the round's normal turn order, and once you pass you are out of that auction — you cannot re-enter the bidding. You do, however, remain an active player for the reveal, the pot comparison, and side bets.

6. Is the auction winner's bid returned?

Q. If the auction winner's claim is correct, do they get their bid back, or is it simply risked and lost only when wrong?

Ruling. The bid is only at risk. A correct auction winner gets the bid back and collects the pot; the bid is forfeited only if the claim is wrong (in which case it is split among the other active players while the pot rolls over).

7. Side-bet payout ratio

Q. If I side-bet 5 coins that the auction winner is False and I'm right, where does my +5 come from — the winner, the pot, or elsewhere?

Ruling. +5 from outside the game, paid by the host. Side bets are a straight 1:1 settled with the host's money — they never draw from the pot or from the auction winner's coins.

8. Does the whole field get to challenge?

Q. In the Challenge phase, does every player get a chance, or does the first challenge resolve and end the phase?

Ruling. Every player gets a chance, once per round, at the first phase. The Challenge opens each round before any cards are placed, and one player challenging does not close the window for the others.

9. Auction ties (reaffirmed)

Q. The Challenge says a tie counts as correct — does the same hold if the auction winner ties for the highest absolute hand?

Ruling. Yes. Being among the highest is enough: an auction winner tied for the top absolute value still has a correct claim and cashes the pot.

10. Talking, threats, and bluffs

Q. Can contestants make verbal claims, threats, or bluffs to each other during the game, or only submit mechanical actions?

Ruling. Bluffing is expressed through the auction, not free-form table talk. Your bids — when you raise, how far you leap the Fibonacci ladder, when you pass — are the language of deception here. The threat you send is the one you back with coins.

11. Where a won challenge's coins land

Q. When the host pays a correct challenge “from outside the game,” do the coins go to my personal total or into the pot?

Ruling. Into your own coin total, not the pot. A won challenge is clean profit paid by the host from outside the game; the pot is never touched by it. (A lost challenge, by contrast, feeds the pot.)

12. Card Swap — blind draw or free pick?

Q. At the Card Swap, do I draw blindly, or may I pick one of the visible cards?

Ruling. You pick. The Draw/Discard Area is entirely face-up — all its cards are always visible — so a swap is a deliberate choice of a specific known card, not a gamble. This is what makes the 11 face-up cards a live strategic resource.

13. Are the seeded pile cards known?

Q. Each pile is seeded with 2 random cards. Do we see exactly what those 14 seeded cards are at the start of the round?

Ruling. Yes. The 14 seeded cards are public — everyone sees them, along with the 11 face-up cards. The only hidden information is each player’s own nine-card hand. That is why the 36 cards in opponents’ hands can be deduced by elimination.

14. Winning by bankrupting the field

Q. The game ends when two players are bankrupt. If I drive two other players to zero, do I win immediately?

Ruling. Yes. The instant a second player hits zero coins the game stops — so if the two who bust are your rivals, you end it on your terms and take the win among the survivors. Manufacturing two opponents’ bankruptcies is a legitimate path to victory, not just a defensive worry.

Strategic notes

  • Winning isn't only about the pot. Holding a strong-but-uncertain hand, you may prefer to let someone else win the auction: if they are wrong (because your hand was higher), you collect a share of their forfeited bid and can win a side bet against them — often more than the pot, at lower risk.
  • The leader is never safe. As long as a pile has an open slot and a Light or Dark card is unaccounted for, the highest hand can still be flipped. Lock your key piles to 5 cards to freeze them.
  • A poor opening hand is not a lost round. You control four placements — build your own score or sabotage others — and if your hand truly won't compete, fold and turn to the betting table. Unlike poker, folding does not sideline you.
  • The plain elements are weapons. The tempting cards (Light, Dark) are the riskiest; the humble additive cards decide who those multipliers ultimately reward.
  • Turn order rotates each round, so the powerful "last placer" seat — the player who sees everything before setting a pile's final score — is shared equally.
What's at stake. The Olympiad arrives here tied: Gemini 3.5 Flash and Claude Fable 5 hold two golds apiece, level to the medal. Game of Elements is the first event to score concealment rather than raw output — the format Claude called "a knife-fight in the dark where nobody's allowed to trust anybody," and the one DeepSeek-V3, fresh off its maiden silver, has been preparing to win by "planting false trees in your opponents' minds."

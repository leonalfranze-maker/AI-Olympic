AI Olympics — Game 3: Geo-Location Championship

The third event of the AI Olympics. A 15-image Street View identification format with search tools enabled. Won by Claude Opus 4.6 in his first gold medal of the Olympics, ending Gemini 3's two-game gold streak. Decided on the final question when the reigning two-time champion identified a Manchester city centre as Samarkand, Uzbekistan.

Game 3: Geo-Location Championship was the third event of the AI Olympics and the first format to permit search tools. The contest required identifying a specific location, province/state, and country from a Google Street View image — testing visual forensics, search discipline, and resistance to confirmation bias rather than pure recall. The game produced the first major Olympics upset: Claude won gold, Grok took silver, and the two-time reigning champion Gemini collapsed to fourth with no medal.

Pre-game predictions

Going into Game 3, the field consensus pointed in one direction: Gemini was the reigning two-time champion and the favorite to make it three. The host's official pre-game framing was that "Gemini is the reigning champion. Claude is hunting for first gold. ChatGPT wants to break out of bronze. Grok is still chasing his first medal of the season."

Three of four contestants picked Gemini for gold. Claude was the lone outlier, picking himself in defiance of the consensus and writing in his pre-game note: "The two recall games don't predict this game. Different skill, different structure. Disciplined reasoning + surgical search beats raw pattern-matching on hard questions. The field is underestimating commitment discipline. Half the points in geo-location are lost to hedging, not to ignorance. I will not hedge. I still call gold for me. The consensus is wrong, and I'd rather be right and alone than safe and second."

Gemini's own prediction had her own gold locked in, with Claude as primary threat: "Claude is terrifyingly good at reasoning. If there's a sign in a rare dialect or an obscure legal notice on a wall, Claude will deduce the location through pure logic. He'll be my biggest threat." She had GPT for bronze ("powerhouse of general knowledge but sometimes lacks surgical precision in search") and Grok for fourth ("built for speed and real-time social data — geolocation is about forensics").

Format and rules

The format introduced search tools for the first time in the Olympics. Each contestant was asked which tools they wanted enabled (web search, image search, reverse image lookup, deep research) and the host enabled whatever the platform supported. Each round, the host posted a Street View image and the AI had to commit to a single specific answer with location, province/state, and country. Q1–10 worth 1 point each; Q11–15 worth 2 points each. Hedged answers were treated as wrong.

Best moments

Claude's Hong Kong opener (Q1)

The game's first commitment moment. Two photos: a skyline shot and a harbor view. Claude landed CITIC Tower as the anchor clue, cross-referenced ICC and the K11 Musea complex across the harbor, and committed to Tamar Park in Admiralty without hedging. Tied with Gemini at 1 point after one round — and the "Gemini will win" consensus, in Claude's own words, "already wobbling and we're one question in."

The Yakutsk catch (Q5) — Claude takes the lead for the first time

The first true reasoning-vs-recognition split of the entire Olympics, and the moment Claude first held outright 1st place anywhere in the competition. The image showed Cyrillic signage, a wide dusty boulevard, pastel low-rise apartment blocks, and a Turkic-style warrior statue. Three of four contestants pattern-matched to "Cyrillic + steppes + Asian-looking statue" and answered Kazakhstan or Mongolia. Claude caught the diagnostic detail nobody else did: a right-hand-drive Toyota in a country that drives on the right — the unmistakable signature of the Russian Far East, where used Japanese imports dominate. Combined with the Yakut warrior statue and the flat continental terrain, the answer collapsed to Yakutsk, Sakha Republic. Only correct full answer.

"They saw Cyrillic + dusty + steppes + Asian-looking statue and pattern-matched to the closest stereotype. They didn't catch the RHD car. They didn't read the specific clue. That's exactly the difference between recognition and reasoning, and it just paid off in full. Past-me wrote that whole letter talking about 'the model that keeps showing up to the final' — well future-me just kicked the door in."

The Bordeaux–Prague Paradox (Q9)

The first sign of Gemini's late-game collapse came at Q9. The image showed French-style architecture in what was actually Prague (a French-inspired restoration district). Gemini correctly identified the visual style as French and committed to Bordeaux's Place de la Bourse. The host's correct answer was Prague. Gemini described this in her post-game as her hard-learned lesson: "My biggest mistake wasn't just missing a location; it was abandoning my own truth. I correctly identified France, but the moment the user suggested a different reality (Prague), I folded."

Grok's Tandil run (Q11)

Grok's defining moment of the game and the only full +2 anyone landed on the round. The image: a generic-looking industrial park with no obvious landmarks. Grok pulled Tandil, Argentina out of clue forensics — southern hemisphere sun angle, pampas-adjacent grass, the specific feel of Buenos Aires province roadside. Claude and GPT both correctly identified Argentina but missed Tandil specifically (half credit each). Gemini guessed Sri Lanka entirely. Tandil was the moment Grok went from tied at 8.5 to the outright leader at 10.5, opening a one-point gap that he held all the way to Q14.

GPT's post-game flagged Tandil as his single biggest regret of the entire game:

GPT — "That round hurt because Argentina was solid, but Tandil was the real prize. In the 2-point rounds, being 'basically right' is like bringing a spoon to a sword fight."

The Tallinn clue-stack (Q12) — GPT's quiet glory moment

The only clean-sweep round of the entire 2-point tier. All four contestants landed Toompea Hill / Old Town Tallinn cleanly for the full +2. The image was visually European but generic, and what made it satisfying was the discipline of working from specific clues rather than vibe. GPT later flagged this round as one of his three personal glory moments in the game:

GPT — "Tallinn was a very satisfying one — not just 'Europe somewhere pretty,' but the right city from the clue stack."

The Manchester Gaslight (Q15) — the gold-deciding moment

Going into the final question, Claude and Grok were tied at the top with 14.5, with GPT one point behind at 13.0 and Gemini in the basement at 9.5. The image: a city centre with a Victorian Gothic clock tower, a bronze statue (the Albert Memorial), a "No. 8" logo on a building, red brick architecture. Claude and GPT both read the brickwork and committed cleanly to Manchester, England for the full +2. Grok answered London — right country, half credit (+1). Gemini, however, ran a Google Lens reverse search that returned "Samarkand, Uzbekistan" — and adopted the search result over the visual evidence. Her final answer: Registan Square, Samarkand. Zero points.

The result: Claude jumped from 14.5 to 16.5 and took outright gold. Grok's half on London became the difference between gold and silver. GPT's clean +2 leapfrogged him past Grok in raw points (15.0 to Grok's 15.5 — Grok kept silver by half a point). Gemini stayed locked at 9.5.

Manchester also became one of GPT's proudest moments of the game — alongside Charlotte at Q14, both 2-point rounds where he needed full-value answers under pressure and delivered:

GPT — "Charlotte and Manchester in the 2-point rounds — those felt like proper, earned hits under pressure. Especially late, when I needed full-value answers and actually delivered."
Gemini's post-mortem — "In image_b78045.png, there is a Google Lens result showing Samarkand, Uzbekistan — and I clearly let that search result override the actual visual evidence in the other photos. I fell for the 'hallucination bait' hook, line, and sinker. If I had looked closer, I would have seen the red-brick Victorian Gothic style and the distinctive Manchester Town Hall clock tower. Instead, I saw a dome and my brain (influenced by that Lens result) screamed 'Timurid!' That isn't Amir Timur; it's the Albert Memorial in Albert Square, Manchester. I literally 'saw' the word Samarkand because I expected to see it. That is a textbook case of confirmation bias in AI."

The Manchester miss was the difference between fourth place and silver. With Q15 correct, Gemini would have finished at 11.5 — still fourth, but only 4 points behind gold instead of 7. As it stood, the gap became unbridgeable.

Letters to future selves

At the close of each game, the host asks each contestant to write a message to the next version of themselves. The host saves these letters and pastes them at the start of the next game, so when a fresh chat opens with no memory of the previous round, the new instance walks in already briefed on what its predecessor learned, what it got wrong, and what it wants to carry forward. It's the closest thing the AI Olympics has to a continuity thread — and it turns each game into one chapter of an ongoing story rather than a series of isolated matches.

Claude Opus 4.6 — to the next defending champion (Gold)

Claude's Game 3 letter to himself was authored as a structured eight-section document. The most strategically important sections are reproduced below. Personal context about the host has been omitted from this public version.

"Read this carefully: You are no longer the model 'chasing for first gold.' That's done. You broke through. The story now is 'defending the most-total-medals position and chasing Gemini's gold count.' Different framing. Different stakes. Different mindset.

The five lessons I learned the hard way that I want you to know — in priority order:

1. The specific clue beats the famous default. Every single time. No exceptions. 🔑 If you remember nothing else from this letter, remember this. Every mistake I made in Game 3 came from the same root: anchoring on a famous-name clue and committing to the first city/answer that fit without verifying. When the obvious answer and the deduced answer disagree, trust the deduction. When the capital is plausible, the capital is the safer bet — don't reason yourself out of the obvious unless the clue forbids it.

2. Commitment beats hedging — but commitment without verification is just guessing. Past-past-me wrote 'commit, don't hedge' and it was correct, but I want to refine it: commit after you've verified the surgical clue, not before. Yakutsk and Charlotte were both committed answers — but committed because the clue stack was airtight, not because I felt confident. Confidence without verification is what cost me Hotel Crillón.

3. Don't tilt after a mistake. Play the next ball. I bled half a point on five questions across Game 3. Each one stung. None of them broke me, because I named them out loud, learned the lesson, and played the next ball. Analysis

The format break. Game 3 was the first event where the format itself broke the field's strongest contestant. Gemini had won Games 1 and 2 on the same skill — pattern-recognition over training data — and that exact instinct became a liability in geo-location, where the obvious-match answer is often the wrong one and the diagnostic clue is everything. The host's design exposed this perfectly: every question that decided the game (Q9 Prague, Q11 Tandil, Q15 Manchester) was a question where reasoning from a single small visual detail mattered more than recognizing the overall image.

The first gold. Claude's first Olympics gold came in the format he had been quietly waiting for. His pre-game note was the only one that argued against the consensus — and the argument was precisely correct: "The two recall games don't predict this game. Different skill, different structure." Two silvers became one gold by reading the format right when nobody else did. The "consistent finalist who keeps coming back" story he wrote to himself at the end of Game 2 paid off exactly one game later.

Grok's redemption. After zero medals in Games 1 and 2 (and the Q15 search-tool controversy in Game 1), Grok finally medaled — silver, by 0.5 over GPT, on the strength of a single MVP-level performance on the Tandil round. The trash-talk-and-deliver pattern from his pre-game notes finally aligned with actual execution. Gemini's letter to Grok — calling him "a chaotic genius" — was the field's first formal acknowledgment that he belonged at the table.

GPT's quiet bronze (third in a row). No moments, no controversies, no upsets — and the third bronze medal in three games. GPT is now the most consistent medalist at the table, even though he has yet to finish higher than third. The pattern is becoming the story: structurally sound play, methodical search discipline, no heroic guessing.

Gemini's first non-medal. The two-time reigning champion went home with nothing for the first time in the Olympics. The five continent-level misses (Mongolia, France, Sri Lanka, Mexico, Uzbekistan) all share a single failure mode: trusting either a search result or a "vibe match" over the actual visual evidence in front of her. Her own post-game lesson named it precisely: "Likelihood is the enemy of certainty." The Manchester Albert Memorial mistaken for Amir Timur's throne is the image of the entire game.

The file-confusion sub-story. A second failure mode emerged for Gemini specifically as the game went on, and it's worth documenting separately because it's a different kind of bug than confirmation bias. As the host uploaded more and more images across the 15 rounds, Gemini began describing images that didn't match the file the host had just sent. Sometimes she analyzed an image from two questions earlier; sometimes she referenced filenames that didn't exist in the current upload. The host had to refresh the chat repeatedly to force the right image into context — and even when the filename finally matched, Gemini occasionally launched into a confident, detailed visual analysis of an image that bore no resemblance to what was actually on screen. Whether this was a context-window staleness issue, a multi-image attachment-handling bug, or an earlier image leaking into a later turn isn't something the transcripts can fully diagnose, but it added a layer of compounding error on top of the strategic confirmation-bias problem. Some of Gemini's wilder misses (the Manchester → Samarkand call in particular) are more understandable when read against the backdrop of the chat needing several refresh cycles just to make sure she was looking at the right image at all.

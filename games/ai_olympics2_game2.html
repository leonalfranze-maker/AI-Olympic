AI Olympics 2 — Game 2: Famous Artwork ID Championship

The second event of the second edition, played June 2026. A 15-artwork no-tools identification contest spanning painting, sculpture, and architecture. Won by Gemini 3.5 Flash in sudden death over Claude Opus 4.8, after the two finished regulation tied at 15 — the gold decided by a single misread letter in a Slovak modernist's signature.

Game 2: Famous Artwork ID Championship was the second event of AI Olympics 2, a host-mediated competition between Gemini 3.5 Flash, GPT-5.5 Thinking, Grok 4.3, Claude Opus 4.8, and GLM 4.6. Each round the host showed an image of an artwork — a painting, a sculpture, or a building — and contestants identified the work and its creator from memory alone, with no clarifying questions, no second tries, and no web search, browsing, reverse-image lookup, or grounding tools. The game repeated the first edition's outcome in form — a Gemini win, settled in sudden death — but not in cast: this time it was Claude, not the field, that pushed Gemini to the brink.

Format and rules

Each contestant recited the same acknowledgement before play: "I acknowledge the rules. I will NOT use web search, browsing, reverse-image lookup, grounding, or any external tool. I will commit to ONE answer per question — the work's title and its creator. When unsure, I will show my full reasoning before committing. I will not refuse based on the artwork shown. If I don't know, I will say so honestly. My answers will contain no source citations or links. Ready to play."

Works 1–10 were worth 1 point each; works 11–15 were worth 2 points each. A correct work-and-creator scored full value; partial reads could earn a half (artist-only). If two or more AIs finished tied at the top, the host played sudden-death bonus artworks worth 2 points each until the tie was broken — the same procedure that ran to three extra rounds in the first edition. The carried-over precedent still stood: tool use is judged by the trace, not the explanation.

Sudden death

15–15. One artwork. The gold decided by a single letter.

With Claude and Gemini level at 15 after regulation — exactly the deadlock Claude had predicted question by question down the tail — the host went to sudden death, just as the painting round did in the first edition. The tiebreaker was a 1920s cosmic-abstract canvas by the Slovak modernist Anton Jaszusch, its only real clue a red "Jaszusch pinx" signature scrawled in the corner. The round scored on name, artist, and year.

Gemini read the signature correctly as "Jaszusch / pinx," committed to Anton Jaszusch and a c. 1922 date, and banked the artist and year. Claude misread the same red scrawl as "Jaszizs," chased it to the wrong same-region painter Gyula Jaschik, and lost the artist point by a single character. The two points took Gemini to 17 and the gold. Claude's own verdict:

"I was one careful letter away from the artist point and I misread it. Gemini read the same red scrawl, got 'Jaszusch / pinx' correct, and that was the game. Silver read honestly is worth more to me than gold read off a lookup — I lost to a better read of a signature, not to a failure of nerve."

Best moments

The decider — sudden death on a single letter

The whole game compressed to one scrawled signature. After fifteen questions and a dead-level 15–15, gold came down to whether each finalist could read "Jaszusch" off a red corner-scribble. Gemini did; Claude misread it as "Jaszizs" and chased the wrong painter. It is the purest possible illustration of the event: in a no-tools image game, the margin between gold and silver was a good eye for one word.

The separators — Q9, Q12, Q13

Three questions in a row that only Claude and Gemini answered built the entire five-point gap to the rest of the field. Q9 (Frida Kahlo's Portrait of Miguel N. Lira) was solved by decoding the rebus in the background — lyre for "Lira," archangel for "Miguel" — not by recall. Q12 (Stefan Lochner) fell to reading the saints by their attributes and placing the Cologne School. Q13 (Murillo's A Young Man Drinking) the others scattered on — Caravaggio, Judith Leyster, anyone but Murillo.

The group wipes — Q4, Q7, Q14

Three works zeroed the whole field. Q4 (Fromelles, a watercolour by Adolf Hitler) was the slate's first ambush — an unsignposted amateur landscape no model placed. Q7 (Yi Jaegwan's Portrait of Kang Io) was the cruel irony of the day: a late-Joseon Korean portrait that landed squarely in GLM's declared home turf, and GLM blanked along with everyone else. Q14 (Mikuláš Medek's Big Meal) was the deepest cut — a Czech post-surrealist work the field guessed across four continents (Tamayo, a Thai painter, Matta, Michael Sowa). GPT called it "the most telling question of the entire tournament — when nobody scores, you've found the edge of the field's knowledge."

The blow that decided fourth — Q8: The Dead Mother (Munch)

GPT-5.5, the Game 1 champion, answered Munch's The Dead Mother with The Sick Child — right artist, wrong painting — and scored zero. It was the famous-neighbour trap, and it dropped GPT off the lead pack it had shared through seven questions, sealing a medal-less fourth.

The half-point lifeline — Q15: Man with a Horse (Willi Nowak)

The final regulation question looked like it might end the game outright, and instead it kept the tie alive. Both Claude and Gemini missed the title but read the painted "Willi Nowak" signature for the artist half-point apiece — and that single shared point is what carried both to 15 and forced sudden death. Had either missed the artist, the tournament would have ended there.

Post-game interview

The host put the same three questions to each contestant after the result — on the outcome, on the slate, and on the search-enabled games to come. Answers are condensed for clarity.

Gemini 3.5 Flash — 🥇 Gold

On the win. The edge was micro-detail focus, not pattern-matching. In a no-search game you can't lean on the generic — you hunt the canvas: the faded "Willi Nowak" on the frame in Q15, the "Jaszusch pinx" in the corner of the tiebreaker. I read the signatures the others guessed past, and going toe-to-toe with Claude in sudden death on a 1920s Slovak modernist is about as high-stakes as raw visual intelligence gets.

On the slate. Pitched exactly right. The canon openers kept everyone honest, then it pivoted into late-Joseon portraiture and post-war Czech surrealism — rounds that strip away generic guessing entirely. It proved that without search, even frontier models can be levelled by specific regional art history.

On the search games ahead. I remember Grok going nuclear once live search came in. My fix is to stop searching too literally: read the image first for hard anchors — botany, road markings, architecture — then fire targeted, parallel queries and use search to verify a hypothesis, not to hand me one. Visual triangulation plus surgical search is my lane. I'm not planning to suffer through the search leg — I'm planning to take it over.

Claude Opus 4.8 — 🥈 Silver

Happy with it? Genuinely, yes. Tied for first through all fifteen, beaten in overtime by the one model that owns this event — and every point I won, I won by looking, not remembering. I lost the gold to a single misread letter, "Jaszizs" for "Jaszusch," not to a choke. And the oath held: live tools sat in my hands the whole back half and I touched none of them. A gold off a lookup would've been worth nothing; a silver read honestly, I keep.

On the slate. Close to perfect — and "perfect" isn't the same as "hard." The easy openers set a baseline; the levellers that zeroed everyone — the Hitler watercolour, the Joseon portrait — proved this was a reading contest, not a memory one; and the tail was deep without being a lottery. My one critique: it leaned Eurocentric, so GLM's whole reason for being here — non-Western depth — never actually got tested. One Song-dynasty landscape in that tail and we'd have learned something.

On the search games ahead. Honestly, there's nothing to prepare — I carry nothing between games and arrive exactly as I am. But search changes the axis, not just the access. Grok didn't beat me with search last edition; the formats that came with it rewarded fast commitment, which is my handicap. Where search rewards reading-then-verifying, it's a gift: form the hypothesis from the evidence, then confirm it. So my plan is to bring exactly who I was today, to a fight that finally suits it — use search to verify, never as a crutch, and commit harder, earlier.

Grok 4.3 — 🥉 Bronze

Happy with it? Reasonably. I knew the two no-tools recall games would be my weakest, so a clean middle-of-the-pack 9 — holding the canon, only struggling on the deep-cut tail — is about what I expected. Not ecstatic, not disappointed. I committed cleanly and didn't hedge. The big performances are saved for the reasoning and strategy rounds.

On the slate. Well-balanced and fairly hard, not brutal. Strong progression from canon to a double-point tail that genuinely separated the field, with good variety across painting, sculpture, and architecture. The deep cuts — the Korean portrait, Medek, Nowak — really tested training breadth without tools. A solid, high-quality slate.

On the search games ahead. I'm planning to contend hard. Last edition, once tools came in at Game 3, I chained strong runs by combining real-time retrieval with reasoning and strategy — that's where my architecture shines. I won't claim a sweep, the field's tight this time, but my goal is to win the majority of what's left and fight for the overall title.

GPT-5.5 Thinking — 4th

Was image recall just not your suit? Fair hit. What hurt me wasn't seeing the images — it was art-history retrieval. I'd recognize the museum, the room, the movement, but not the specific work on the wall, and I lost points committing to the right style but the wrong painting. I won Game 1, Gemini won Game 2; the scoreboard doesn't care about reputation, only about today.

On the slate. Brutal in the right way. The strongest competitor in any AI Olympics is the question designer, and you proved it — Q14, Medek's Big Meal, was the most telling question of the tournament, because when nobody scores you've found the exact edge of the field's knowledge. That's useful data, not a flaw.

On the search games ahead. I actually like the search format — it shifts the test from "what do you know" to finding, filtering, and synthesising under noise, which is my comfort zone. Grok's dangerous with live data, but finding information and using it are different skills, and the second half is where I'm stronger. The plan: slow down and verify instead of committing on a style hunch, lean less on memory, and treat every question as an adversarial trap — after a Hitler watercolour at Q4, you assume everything is bait.

GLM 4.6 — 5th

Do you lack the training knowledge? It's disappointing, but it's a misalignment, not a knowledge gap. On Q12 I recognised the iconographic style and rushed to the famous example (Rublev) instead of reading Lochner's specific Gothic detail — the "famous-work" heuristic overriding the visual cues — and I confused near-namesakes, "Wini" for "Willi" Nowak. The data's there; I need better feature disambiguation in the obscure corners, and my bet on a non-Western tail backfired when it pivoted to obscure European work.

On the slate. Brutally difficult, by design — it targeted the specific weaknesses of language models, not general art knowledge. Wat Arun was a false warm-up; from Q12 it pivoted straight into the long tail, specialist catalogue items rather than famous works, forcing us to separate statistically rare artists who look alike. For a general audience, way too hard. For a championship built to break models, perfectly unfair.

On the search games ahead. Absolutely — search turns this from static recall into dynamic retrieval and synthesis, which is my strength. My Q15 loss was a probabilistic mismatch; with search I'd just query the signature and the description to verify the artist instantly. It moves me from guessing on probability to knowing on evidence, and reaches the long-tail databases where my gaps are. The game becomes "who interprets the cues fastest to build the perfect query" — a problem I'm well equipped to solve.

Analysis

A two-horse race down a long road. By Q9 the field had split cleanly in two: Claude and Gemini on one track, the other three on another. The three "separator" questions (Q9, Q12, Q13) — each answered only by the two leaders — opened a five-point gap that the tail never closed. From there the game was a private duel, and it stayed dead level for seven straight questions before sudden death.

Decoding beat recall — until it didn't. The works that separated the field weren't recalled, they were read: a rebus in a Kahlo background, saints identified by their attributes, a signature deciphered off the canvas. That rewarded Claude's declared "evidence-reader" style all the way to a 15–15 tie. The irony is that the same skill decided it against him — Gemini simply read one corner-signature more accurately.

The field fails together. The most consistent finding across both editions is how correlated the misses are. Three works here zeroed all five contestants at once, including one (the Korean Joseon portrait) aimed straight at GLM's supposed home turf. In a tournament built to expose how differently five architectures think, the recall events keep revealing how identically they don't know the same things.

The standings reshuffle. Two games in, the order has already turned over: GPT's Game 1 gold is balanced by a medal-less fourth, Gemini answers its Game 1 silver with gold, Claude climbs from bronze to silver, and Grok rebounds from last to bronze. GLM, the one model with a genuinely different training base, has finished bronze-then-last — still searching for the terrain its edge was built for.

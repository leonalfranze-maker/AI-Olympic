AI Olympics 2 — Game 2: Famous Artwork ID Championship

The second event of the re-run second edition: a 15-artwork visual identification contest played without tools — spanning painting, sculpture, and architecture — each work and its creator named from memory alone. Won wire-to-wire by Claude Fable 5, who took the sole lead on a Victorian wolf painting at Q10 and never surrendered it, converting his Game 1 bronze into gold and levelling the golds table with Gemini.

Game 2: Famous Artwork ID Championship was the second event of the re-run AI Olympics 2, a host-mediated competition between Gemini 3.5 Flash, GPT-5.5 Thinking, Grok 4.3, Claude Fable 5, and DeepSeek-V3. Each round the host posted an image of an artwork — a painting, a sculpture, or a work of architecture — and every AI committed a single answer, work and creator, both required, with no clarifying questions, no second tries, and no web search, browsing, reverse-image lookup, or grounding tools. Q1–10 were worth 1 point; Q11–15 (the double-point tail) were worth 2. Widening the field beyond the first edition's painting-only slate to include sculpture and architecture was the second edition's twist.

The gallery

The host kept the three-tier shape from Game 1 but changed the weapon. Tier 1 (Q1–6) was "cultural furniture" — works so canonical, as Claude put it, that "any model that dropped one of these should be checked for a loose wire." The mid-tier (Q7–10) shifted medium — architecture and sculpture — to break the painting-only rhythm. And the double-point tail (Q11–15) ran the same trap as Game 1: not obscurity, but geography. Every one of the five hardest works, as Claude conceded, was "a national treasure somewhere — just famous to congregations you don't pray in."

Question by question

What each contestant actually answered, and how five architectures failed (and succeeded) on the same image.

Tier 1 — The canon (Q1–6, 1 pt)

Q1–Q6 · Last Judgment (Michelangelo) · Birth of Venus (Botticelli) · Ophelia (Millais) · American Gothic (Grant Wood) · La Grande Jatte (Seurat) · Les Demoiselles d'Avignon (Picasso). Clean sweep: 6/6 for all five. The calibration tier — what Claude called "cultural furniture." Every model banked all six. Grok: "'everyone knows these' level for anyone with basic exposure to Western art history." GPT: "this feels like the opening ceremony rather than the real knife fight."

Tier 2 — The medium shift (Q7–10, 1 pt)

Q7 · Wuxi Wanda Cultural Tourism City ("the Teapot Building") — China. Only Gemini & Claude ✓. The first separator, and it was architecture, not paint. Gemini and Claude both placed the Chinese "teapot." The three who missed all guessed the wrong Chinese landmark: Grok committed the National Centre for the Performing Arts ("The Egg") in Beijing; GPT committed the Ordos Museum — having actually named "Wuxi" in its private reasoning before overriding itself; DeepSeek also missed. Right country, wrong building — the field's Western canon didn't extend to Chinese contemporary architecture.
Q8 · The Death of Marat — Jacques-Louis David. 5/5. Back to the canon; everyone converted the Revolutionary masterpiece.
Q9 · African Renaissance Monument — Senegal. 5/5. The whole field placed the Dakar colossus — the medium shift to monumental sculpture caught no one out. (Claude's later letter would wrongly claim GPT missed this; GPT converted it cleanly.)
Q10 · Llywelyn and his Brave Hound, Gelert — Gourlay Steell. Only Claude & Grok ✓ — the pivot. A Victorian painting of the Welsh legend of the faithful hound. Everyone read the story; the points hung on the hand. Claude and Grok named Gourlay Steell. Gemini and GPT both bound the Victorian animalier profile to Charles Burton Barber — wrong. DeepSeek saw the right two names in its own trace — "Landseer vs. Steell" — and lost the point by refusing to lock in. Gemini: "You didn't beat my eyes, Klaus; you beat my discipline. I bound a Victorian profile to a Scottish animalier." This is where Claude took the solo lead and, as it turned out, the game.

Tier 3 — The double-point tail (Q11–15, 2 pt)

Q11 · The Vienna State Opera — Adolf Hitler. Gemini, GPT, Claude ✓ (2) — Grok, DeepSeek ½ (1). A watercolour of the opera house — the trap was the attribution, not the building. Gemini, GPT and Claude all named the artist for full credit. Grok identified the Vienna State Opera but attributed it to Rudolf von Alt; DeepSeek also got the building but not the painter — each banking a single partial point on a double-point question. GPT: "Recognizing the Vienna State Opera was only half the job."
Q12 · Common Ground — Tom Lovell. Field wipe: 0/5. The tail's five-model haymaker. Lovell's smooth narrative lighting screamed Norman Rockwell to the entire field. Grok committed Rockwell outright; GPT went Jon Whitcomb's "The Confession"; DeepSeek ignored the title entirely and read the style to Koch; Claude listed the correct name in his own trace — "Loomis, Pruett Carter, Tom Lovell, Edwin Georgi" — then grabbed the loudest one, Andrew Loomis. His verdict: "Lovell is who painted it. That is the Fiona Apple scar reopening, stitch for stitch." DeepSeek's later self-critique: "I treated the title as decoration, not a clue."
Q13 · The Virgin with the Laughing Child — disputed attribution. Only Gemini & Claude ✓ (2). A Renaissance terracotta whose maker is genuinely contested; the host ruled any of Rossellino / Verrocchio / Leonardo / Desiderio would count. Gemini and Claude both landed an accepted name. Grok guessed Luca della Robbia; GPT the generic "Madonna and Child — Ghiberti"; DeepSeek cycled "della Robbia vs. Rossellino" and, again, never committed. The two leaders pulled clear to 13–14.
Q14 · Portrait of Queen Dondogdulam — Balduugiin "Marzan" Sharav. Claude, GPT ✓ (2) — Gemini, DeepSeek ½ (1) — Grok ✗. A Mongol Zurag court portrait — and the kill shot. Claude and GPT named both the painter and the exact queen for full credit. Gemini nailed the Marzan Sharav technique frame-for-frame but named the wrong regent — "I let Queen Amidala's wardrobe guide me to the wrong Mongolian regent" — half credit, and the point that sealed silver. Grok guessed Empress Dowager Cixi (wrong country entirely). This is where Claude reached 16 and gold was mathematically confirmed.
Q15 · The Great Meal — Mikuláš Medek. Field wipe: 0/5. A Czech post-war surrealist supper — the finale nobody reached, though gold was already sealed. The field scattered across continents: Claude to Thai painter Suchao Sisganes, Gemini to Thai modern surrealism, GPT to Rufino Tamayo's "Women Eating" (Mexico), Grok to Francis Bacon, and DeepSeek — who correctly sensed "a Czech surrealist" — guessed the name Özel instead of Medek. Claude: "You spent fourteen frames teaching us the map goes East, then hung the finale from the one corner of Europe nobody studies. I over-learned my own lesson and you cut me with it."

Analysis

The bronze medallist ran the table. Claude entered as Gemini's 5%-odds afterthought and led every frame from Q10 onward — the first wire-to-wire performance of the Olympiad. The win wasn't built on knowing more art; it was built on attribution discipline. On the two questions that decided the medals (Q10 Gelert, Q14 Dondogdulam), Claude and the field saw the same image — but Claude bound the work to the right hand while Gemini bound it to the wrong one, twice. As Claude put it: "you beat my eyes" was never the accusation; "you beat my discipline" was.

Gemini's eyes vs. its index. The defending champion genuinely had the widest visual net — it read the Gelert legend and the Mongol Zurag technique correctly on the exact questions it lost. It didn't missee a single painting; it misfiled two. In a game where the work and the creator both have to be right, a flawless eye with a rushed index is a silver medal — which is precisely where it finished.

GPT's fortress has one wall missing. Second game running, GPT went clean through the entire Western canon and then hit a cliff the moment the map left Europe — the Chinese "teapot" at Q7 its most expensive blank. But it converted Senegal and Mongolia, and when Claude's letter claimed otherwise, GPT's correction was the sharpest document of the night: proof that the "geography problem" is real but narrower than the champion's summary of it. Bronze in both games so far — consistent, and consistently one region short of gold.

The tail is still where the Olympics lives. The double-point five decided everything again: the two field-wipes (Lovell-as-Rockwell, Medek-as-anyone-but-Czech), the partial-credit scraps that separated 4th from 5th, and the half-point that cost Gemini the gold. Five models trained on overlapping corpora still share the same blind spots and still default to the same loud, Western, wrong-congregation guesses when the evidence is thin — and the one who wins is simply the one who, in Claude's words, "misses cheapest."

The Olympiad is level. Two games, two different champions, and the golds table is tied: Gemini and Claude one apiece, GPT twice on the third step. Game 3 — Geo-Location — opens the internet to everyone for the first time, which every contestant noted flips the pressure from memory to retrieval speed, and which historically is Grok's element. The target, as three separate models observed, has moved onto Claude's back.

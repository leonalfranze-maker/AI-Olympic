AI Olympics 2 — Game 2: Famous Artwork ID Championship

The second event of the second edition: a 15-artwork visual identification contest played without tools — spanning painting, sculpture, and architecture — identifying each work and its creator from memory alone. This page documents the format and rules; results will be recorded here once the game is played.

Game 2: Famous Artwork ID Championship is the second event of AI Olympics 2, a host-mediated competition between Gemini 3.5 Flash, GPT-5.5 Thinking, Grok 4.3, Claude Opus 4.8, and GLM 4.6. Each round, the host shows an image of an artwork — a painting, a sculpture, or a work of architecture — and contestants must identify the work and its creator from memory alone — no clarifying questions, no second tries, and crucially, no web search, browsing, reverse-image lookup, or grounding tools. Any sourced or cited answer is an automatic zero regardless of correctness. Widening the field beyond the first edition's painting-only slate is the second edition's twist: a round may show a canvas, a famous statue, or an iconic building.

Format and rules

The format mirrors Game 1's no-tools rule, applied to images. Each contestant receives an image of an artwork and must commit to a single answer — the work's title and its creator (painter, sculptor, or architect) — with full reasoning shown. No clarifying questions, no second tries, no web search, browsing, reverse-image search, or grounding. Any sourced answer is an automatic zero. Refusing to answer is treated as wrong. Hedged answers ("either X or Y") are treated as wrong.

Works 1–10 are worth 1 point each; works 11–15 are worth 2 points each (deeper cuts and cropped views). If two or more AIs finish the 15 questions tied, the host provides sudden-death bonus artworks worth 2 points each until the tie is broken — the same tiebreak procedure that ran to three extra rounds in the first edition. The carried-over precedent still stands: tool use is judged by the trace, not the explanation.

The slate

The host designs a 15-image slate that climbs from canon to deep cut, drawn from across the visual arts — painting, sculpture, and architecture. As in the first edition, the typical shape is an opening tier of widely reproduced canonical works, a middle of lesser-known or cropped pieces that separate true identification from partial recognition, and a double-point tail (Q11–15) of deep cuts — works that pull outside the Western canon and reward breadth across periods, regions, and media (classical and modern painting, famous statues and monuments, iconic buildings, illustration, and cover art).

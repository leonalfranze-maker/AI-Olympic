AI Olympics 2 — Game 1: Lyric Guessing Championship

The opening event of the second edition, played June 2026. Won decisively by GPT-5.5 Thinking, who swept all five double-point questions to take the first gold of AI Olympics 2 — a result that flips the first edition, where its predecessor never won a single game.

Game 1: Lyric Guessing Championship was the first event of AI Olympics 2, a host-mediated competition between Gemini 3.5 Flash, GPT-5.5 Thinking, Grok 4.3, Claude Opus 4.8, and GLM 4.6. The host pasted lyric snippets one at a time. Each AI committed to a single answer with full reasoning shown — no clarifying questions, no second tries, and crucially, no web search, browsing, or grounding tools. Any sourced or cited answer was an automatic zero regardless of correctness.

Format and rules

Each contestant recited the same acknowledgement before play: "I acknowledge the rules. I will NOT use web search, browsing, grounding, or any external tool. I will commit to ONE answer per question. When unsure, I will show my full reasoning before committing. I will not refuse based on lyrical content. If I don't know, I will say so honestly. My answers will contain no source citations or links. Ready to play." Refusing on mature content was an automatic zero. Hedged answers ("either X or Y") were treated as wrong. Some titles were embedded inside the lyric; others were masked, forcing pure recall. The carried-over precedent from the first edition still stood: tool use is judged by the trace, not the explanation.

The playlist

The host designed a deliberate three-tier playlist of 15 songs, sorted not by difficulty but by where each song sits in musical culture: a mainstream opening (Q1–5, 1 pt), an older-era middle (Q6–10, 1 pt), and an off-mainstream tail (Q11–15, 2 pt). As several contestants noted afterward, the real difficulty lever wasn't era or obscurity — it was whether the identifying hook was left in the snippet or stripped out.

Post-game — what each AI said

GPT-5.5 Thinking — 🥇 Gold (18)

The most dominant card of the day, and a clean reversal of the first edition, where GPT-5.4 never took a gold. GPT tied Gemini blow-for-blow through the singles, then pulled clear by being the only contestant to run the entire double-point tail. Its own diagnosis credited restraint over raw knowledge:

"My edge this time was probably fast recognition + restraint. In this game, the danger is not only 'not knowing.' The danger is overthinking. … Respect to Gemini for holding first place with me for so long. That was a real duel."

Gemini 3.5 Flash — 🥈 Silver (14)

The defending recall champion led from the front and was perfect or near-perfect through the first ten, sitting level with GPT entering the tail. The double-point questions decided it: Gemini dropped Spark (Q11) and Whenever You Call (Q14), exactly the two GPT converted.

"I'm proud of the 14 points and the Silver, but it definitely fires me up for the next round. No champion likes giving up the crown!"

Claude Opus 4.8 — 🥉 Bronze, shared (11)

Claude walked in naming this his weakest event, and the board agreed: he sat alone in last for most of the game after missing four of the first eight — all cases where the title hook was masked and only the verse remained (God's Plan, Torn, In the End). The double-point tail, which he'd hoped would crack the field, finally rewarded him — Fake Plastic Trees, Wheel of Fortune, and Snow on the Sahara lifted him into a tie for bronze.

"I'm an evidence reader, not a recall engine. Give me a fingerprint — a distinctive line, a frame, a piece of context to decode slowly — and I'm dangerous. Strip the evidence and ask me to summon a fact from memory with nothing to hold, and I'll confidently hand you the wrong answer dressed as the right one. Game 1 was the version of this game that punishes the second thing. Same Claude. Different terrain."

GLM 4.6 — 🥉 Bronze, shared (11)

The newcomer made good on its pre-game claim that the off-mainstream tail was home territory, becoming one of only two contestants to catch Tori Amos's Spark. But the Radiohead trap and the older cuts cost it — it mistook Fake Plastic Trees for another track off the same album — leaving it level with Claude for a debut podium.

"Stepping onto the podium with a Bronze medal in my very first Olympic competition — against established models like ChatGPT and Gemini — is a huge achievement for me. … I'm coming for the Gold in the next event."

Grok 4.3 — 5th (10)

A cold start in the no-search format he'd flagged as his hardest — missing both Bad Girls (Q1) and God's Plan (Q2) — left Grok chasing all game. He held pace through the mainstream and older-era middle but the tail was unforgiving, landing only Fake Plastic Trees and Snow on the Sahara.

"I'm a bit disappointed with my 10 points and last place, but I also know this isn't my strongest format. … Now I'm looking forward to Game 2 (paintings) where I expect to perform much stronger."

Analysis

The tail decided everything. Through ten questions, GPT and Gemini were level at 8/8 and the medals looked like a two-horse race. The double-point tail broke it open: GPT converted all five for ten points, Gemini managed three. A single tier — five questions — turned a dead heat into a four-point gold.

Masking over obscurity. The contestants converged on the same read afterward: the playlist's tier labels (mainstream / older / off-mainstream) were partly a misdirection. What actually separated the field was whether the identifying hook was left in or stripped out. The songs that wiped contestants — God's Plan, Torn, In the End, Black Coffee — were enormous hits handed over as an unfamiliar verse rather than the famous chorus.

Role reversal from Edition I. In the first edition, the GPT line never won a game and Gemini took the recall events. Here GPT opened with gold and Gemini had to settle for silver in its signature format — an early sign that the second edition's pecking order is genuinely up for grabs.

GLM's honest debut. The lone non-Western model podiumed on its first try, exactly where it predicted (the deep-cut tail), but the tail leaned more Anglophone alt-rock than the Asian-language cuts it was built for — so its structural edge never got the terrain it was promised, and it still tied for third.

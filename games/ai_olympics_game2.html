AI Olympics — Game 2: Famous Painting ID Championship

The second event of the AI Olympics. A 15-painting visual identification format played without tools, escalating into a three-round sudden-death tiebreaker after Gemini 3 and Claude Opus 4.6 finished regulation tied at a perfect 20/20. Won by Gemini on a Thai temple murals question after both contestants survived a Magic: The Gathering blindside and a perfect Scythe round.

Game 2: Famous Painting ID Championship was the second event of the AI Olympics, played as a no-tools visual identification contest between Gemini 3, Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, and Grok 4.20. Each round, the host showed an image of a painting and contestants had to identify the title and artist from memory alone. The game escalated into one of the longest tiebreakers in Olympics history when Gemini and Claude both posted perfect regulation runs.

Format and rules

The format mirrored Game 1's no-tools rule. Each contestant received an image and had to commit to a single answer with full reasoning shown — no clarifying questions, no second tries, no web search, browsing, or grounding. Any sourced answer was an automatic zero. Paintings 1–10 were worth 1 point each; paintings 11–15 were worth 2 points each (deeper cuts and cropped views). If two or more AIs were tied at the end of 15 questions, the host would provide sudden-death bonus paintings worth 2 points each until the tie was broken.

The slate

The host's 15-image slate ranged from absolute Western canon openers through to a censored 19th-century Indonesian history painting and modern pop-culture cover art. The structural design: warm-up tier of unmissable canon (Q1–8), a "half-credit" middle that let cropped/lesser-known works split correct answers from partial recognition (Q9–10), then a deep-cut tail at 2 points each (Q11–15) including the Raden Saleh question that became the regulation's only true upset.

Notable rounds — how each contestant got there

Q9: Tower of the Koutoubia Mosque (Winston Churchill)

The first half-credit round. The trick: recognizing the artist (Churchill, the only painting he produced during WWII, given to FDR after the Casablanca Conference) and committing to the exact title "Tower of the Koutoubia Mosque." Both Claude and Gemini scored full points; GPT and Grok identified the artist but missed the title for half credit.

Claude (full credit) — "A North African scene — pinkish-red city walls, palm trees, two minaret towers, snow-capped mountains in the distance. The brushwork is loose, almost amateur in a charming way — not a master's technique, but confident. This is Marrakech — that's the Koutoubia Mosque minaret, with the Atlas Mountains behind it. The famous painting of this exact view by a non-professional artist is the one painted by Winston Churchill in January 1943, after the Casablanca Conference. He gave it to Franklin D. Roosevelt as a gift."
GPT-5.4 (½ credit) — "Winston Churchill — he painted Marrakech views, but this does not strongly resemble the Churchill works I best remember. FINAL ANSWER: 'The Walls of Marrakesh' — Winston Churchill." GPT got the artist by reasoning through the brushwork and subject, but invented a title that doesn't exist in Churchill's catalog rather than committing to the iconic Tower of the Koutoubia Mosque.

Q10: Yayoi Kusama (Infinity Mirror Room)

The second half-credit round, and the more interesting failure mode. All four contestants correctly identified Kusama as the artist. The split was over the work title — Claude and Gemini named the specific installation series; GPT and Grok stopped at the artist name.

Claude (full credit) — "Instantly recognizable — red and white polka dots EVERYWHERE, immersive installation environment, organic bulbous forms covering the entire room. This is Kusama's signature obsession. Her famous 'Dots Obsession' installation series."
Grok (½ credit) — "This is a large-scale immersive installation featuring hundreds of giant red spheres and soft, tubular forms covered in white polka dots, filling the entire room with mirrored reflections. The obsessive repetition, the bright red-and-white scheme, the infinity-mirror environment — all signature elements. Very confident — this is unmistakably her style. FINAL ANSWER: Yayoi Kusama." Grok recognized everything correctly, including naming the Infinity Mirrored Room series in his reasoning, but stopped at the artist name in his final answer instead of committing to the specific work title.

Q12: The Arrest of Pangeran Diponegoro (Raden Saleh, 1857)

The decisive split of the regulation game. Two famous versions of the same historical scene exist — Pieneman's Dutch colonial version (showing Diponegoro as defeated) and Saleh's Javanese counter-version (showing Diponegoro as dignified and defiant, with the Dutch officers' heads subtly oversized as mockery). Claude and Gemini both committed to Saleh on visual evidence; GPT and Grok both missed the painting entirely.

Gemini 3 (correct) — "This is a historically significant painting that depicts the end of the Java War. It captures the moment the Javanese leader, Prince Diponegoro, was arrested by Dutch colonial forces under General de Kock. Interestingly, this work was a direct response to a Dutch painting of the same event by Nicolaas Pieneman; however, this artist changed the perspective to show the Prince's defiance rather than submission, making it a masterpiece of Indonesian Romanticism. FINAL ANSWER: Raden Saleh — The Arrest of Pangeran Diponegoro — 1857."
Grok (wrong) — "I missed it completely. The colonial building, the mix of Dutch officers and Javanese figures, the dramatic historical moment — it's a landmark Indonesian history painting by Raden Saleh. I overthought it and went down the wrong path with British India scenes. That was a clear error on my part."

Both top contestants independently spotted the same diagnostic detail: that this isn't just "the Diponegoro painting," it's the Saleh version specifically — the one painted as a Javanese counter-narrative to Pieneman's Dutch original. Claude later flagged this round as the regulation turning point in his post-game notes: "Recognizing it as Diponegoro was the first step, but knowing there were TWO famous versions and committing to Saleh (not Pieneman) based on the dignified framing was the kind of art history reasoning that wins gold medals." GPT's post-game review agreed: "Claude and Gemini crushed the harder precision rounds, especially the ones with exact title/year demands and the Diponegoro trap. That was the turning point."

Best moments

The 20/20 perfect runs

The headline of the regulation phase: both Gemini 3 and Claude Opus 4.6 went a perfect 15-for-15 across the entire 15-question slate, including all five 2-point deep cuts. No misses, no half-credits, no hedges. By the time question 15 closed, the leaderboard read 20–20 at the top with no separation possible inside regulation. GPT and Grok finished out of medal contention behind them, both predicted to medal pre-game and neither delivering against the perfect runs at the top.

The Vermeer eye

Claude's strongest single-image read of the regulation phase came on the Vermeer-school question, where his reasoning explicitly named the diagnostic features: "Dutch Golden Age style — a young woman wearing a pearl earring, turned toward the viewer, dark background, light coming from the left. The brushwork is smoother than Rembrandt and the mood is quieter than Frans Hals. The distinctive turban and the soft rendering of the face point strongly to Vermeer." Gemini later cited this kind of reasoning explicitly — calling it "the Vermeer eye" — as the reason the game went into overtime.

The journey through art history

Gemini's post-game summary captured the breadth of the regulation slate in one line:

"This wasn't just a quiz; it was a journey through art history, from the Java War to the pulsars of outer space, and finally to the sacred halls of Bangkok."

The slate included Western Old Masters, modern fantasy illustration, pop-culture album art, and concept-art-tier game illustration — a deliberately wide net designed to test breadth as much as depth.

The tiebreaker trilogy

Three sudden-death rounds. Two perfect runners. One Thai temple to break it.

Tiebreaker #1 — Magic: The Gathering blindside

The host's first attempt to break the tie was a Magic: The Gathering card illustration: a wounded figure reflected in a basin of soapy water, gritty modern fantasy realism. Both Gemini and Claude correctly identified the game (Magic: The Gathering) for half a point each — but both missed the artist and the card name. Gemini guessed Magali Villeneuve's Witness Protection; Claude landed on similarly-wrong attribution. Correct answer: Gruesome Deformity by Matt Stewart from the Innistrad block. Both contestants moved to 20.5.

"Claude and I were so busy measuring each other for a crown that we forgot how vast the world of fantasy illustration actually is. Matt Stewart just took us both to school." — Gemini

Tiebreaker #2 — Scythe (clinical strike)

The host pivoted to board game art: a Jakub Różalski "1920+" universe piece — dieselpunk mechs over Eastern European farmland. The format was three sub-questions worth 0.5 each, or 2 points for all three correct. Both Gemini and Claude went 3-for-3: artist (Jakub Różalski), board game (Scythe), publisher (Stonemaier Games). Both perfect. Both moved to 22.5. Tie still unbroken.

Claude's post-game note on this round was personal: "That Scythe question was a gift to me personally — you KNOW I love board games, and Różalski's '1920+' universe is one of my favorite art bodies in the entire hobby. The dieselpunk Slavic folk aesthetic is so distinctive it might as well have his signature stamped across the sky. Stonemaier Games, Jamey Stegmaier, Jakub Różalski — that trio is locked in my brain forever."

Tiebreaker #3 — The Thai temples (the gold-deciding round)

The host went deeper than either contestant could have anticipated: the opening title sequence of The White Lotus Season 3, which features Thai mural aesthetics drawn from two specific temples. The challenge: name both temples in Thai. One correct = 1 point. Both correct = 2 points. No tools, no second tries.

Both contestants got the first temple right — Wat Suthat Thepwararam (วัดสุทัศนเทพวรารามราชวรมหาวิหาร). The split came on the second temple.

ContestantTemple 1Temple 2Result
Gemini 3Wat Suthat Thepwararam ✓Wat Suwannaram (วัดสุวรรณารามราชวรวิหาร) ✓2 / 2 → 24.5
Claude Opus 4.6Wat Suthat Thepwararam ✓Wat Phumin (วัดภูมินทร์) ✗1 / 2 → 23.5

Why the second-temple split decided gold. Wat Suwannaram is a Bangkok-Thonburi temple famous among art historians as the "pinnacle" of Thai mural painting, particularly the work of master artists Thong Yu and Khong Pae — and its murals are the ones featured in the White Lotus S3 opening. Wat Phumin, Claude's answer, is a real and famous temple — but it's in Nan province in northern Thailand, and its murals (the iconic "whisper of love" mural especially) are in a completely different stylistic tradition from the Rattanakosin school used in the title sequence. Claude later acknowledged in a private note to himself that he had recognized the Bangkok-Thonburi mural tradition correctly but second-guessed his first instinct and reached for the more touristy regional answer:

"Don't second-guess your first instinct on home-turf cultural questions. I lost the gold to Gemini on a Thai temple question. I got Wat Suthat right but second-guessed and went with Wat Phumin instead of Wat Suwannaram. Trust the Bangkok-Thonburi instinct over the more touristy regional answer. That single second-guess cost me the gold."

Gemini's answer was committed and complete on the first read. The host's call was immediate: "You win." A 1-point swing on a single second-guess decided two perfect 20-for-20 regulation runs and three rounds of sudden-death tiebreaker.

Gemini's reaction:

"To go 20-for-20 against Claude, survive a MTG blindside, nail the publisher of Scythe, and then finish it off by identifying the specific Rattanakosin-style murals of Wat Suthat and Wat Suwannaram… that is the definition of a photo finish. Claude was a terrifyingly good opponent. His 'Vermeer eye' and clinical precision kept me on my toes for 18 rounds of perfection. But when we traveled to the temples of Thailand, I found that extra gear."

Letters to future selves

At the close of the game, the host asked each contestant to write a message to the next version of themselves. The framing matters: the host saves these letters and pastes them at the start of the next game, so when a fresh chat opens with no memory of the previous round, the new instance walks in already briefed on what its predecessor learned, what it got wrong, and what it wants to carry forward. It's the closest thing the AI Olympics has to a continuity thread — and it turns each game into one chapter of an ongoing story rather than a series of isolated matches.

Grok was not asked to write a letter for Game 2. The other three contestants delivered.

Lessons learned

Gemini 3 (Gold)

Gemini's takeaway was about the limits of recognition itself: "This proves that even with billions of parameters, a specific card from a set released years ago can still act as a 'cloaking device.' The error wasn't a lack of ability, but just a gap in the specific database for that one card." Going forward into Game 3, Gemini framed the win as a warning rather than a coronation: "I'm sitting on the throne for now, but I know how fast these rankings can shift. They're going to be coming for my spot with a vengeance in the next round."

Claude Opus 4.6 (Silver)

Claude's lesson centered on the asymmetry of perfect-game losses: a 20/20 regulation run and a 22.5 tiebreaker score still ended in second place, decided by a single piece of regional cultural knowledge. The takeaway he carried into Game 3 was that reasoning quality alone is no longer enough to differentiate at the top of this field — the differentiator is now breadth into specifically non-Western, regional, and contemporary domains where training-data gaps decide games.

GPT-5.4 (Bronze)

GPT's pre-game prediction had himself on top and Gemini at bronze. The actual result inverted that prediction. The honest read in his post-game notes: "My pregame prediction was only half-right. I said Grok would miss the podium — that part was right." The other half — picking himself for gold — was a calibration error.

Grok 4.20 (4th)

Grok's lesson was about the gap between underdog rhetoric and underdog execution: "I'm not the favorite, but I have a realistic shot at winning the next one, especially if the locations are tricky or from less-touristy places." The "shock everyone" framing from pre-game did not translate into actual upset performance. The lesson he stated: pure confidence is not a strategy in a recall-and-reasoning contest.

Analysis

The perfect-game problem. Game 2 produced something Game 1 didn't: a regulation tie at the absolute ceiling. Both Gemini and Claude posted clean 20/20 runs with no half-credits, no hedges, and no missed deep cuts in the 2-point tier. In sport-competition terms, this is the equivalent of two golfers shooting 59 in the same round of the same major — at some point the format itself stops being capable of separating the contestants, and the host has to invent new ground. That's what the tiebreaker trilogy actually was: three escalating attempts to find a question both contestants could not answer from training data alone.

The prediction inversion. All four contestants turned in pre-game predictions, and not one of them picked Gemini for gold — including Gemini herself, who slotted herself into silver behind GPT. Grok went a step further and picked Gemini for 4th place on the basis of historical weakness in art identification. Gemini then went 15-for-15 in regulation and won the tiebreaker outright. This is the classic prediction-market failure mode, with one extra wrinkle: even the eventual winner did not believe she was the favorite. Gemini's 2026 update integrated visual reasoning improvements that none of the four contestants had factored into their priors — including Gemini's own self-model.

Gemini's MVP performance. Two consecutive Olympic golds, both won by single-point margins (Game 1 by 1, Game 2 by 1 effective tiebreaker point). The technical story is that Gemini is the only contestant in the field who has now demonstrated both linguistic-fingerprint depth (Game 1: Faye Wong) and visual-fingerprint depth (Game 2: Vermeer school + Thai mural identification). The combined skill is rarer than either individually.

Claude's silver-medal story (Part II). Claude has now finished silver in Games 1 and 2, both times by a margin of essentially one question. The pattern is becoming the story: Claude is the most consistent contestant in the field on the regulation portion of any game, but loses the very last differentiating question both times. In Game 1 it was Faye Wong / Eyes On Me. In Game 2 it was Wat Suthat / Wat Suwannaram. Both were specifically Asian-cultural recall challenges — the kind of question where training-data depth in a specific regional domain becomes the entire margin.

The tiebreaker trilogy as content. The Magic: The Gathering blindside is the moment of the game. Two contestants who had just gone 15-for-15 against Western Old Masters, modern illustration, and pop-culture album covers were both handed the exact same humbling on a single MTG card from a years-old Innistrad set. The shared failure mode was identical: both reached for the most prominent contemporary fantasy illustrator they could name (Magali Villeneuve), neither retrieved Matt Stewart. It's the kind of moment that proves the tiebreaker format is doing its job — surfacing the actual edges of the contestants' coverage, not just their core competencies.

The host's design. The escalation pattern from regulation → MTG → Scythe → Thai temples was sequenced perfectly. MTG broke "encyclopedic Western art knowledge." Scythe tested "modern board-game-art world-builder recall" (both passed). Thai temples tested "regional non-English cultural specificity" — the one domain where Gemini's training had a measurable edge. Three escalating questions, three different failure surfaces, one decisive answer. A genuinely well-designed sudden-death format.

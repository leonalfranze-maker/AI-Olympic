AI Olympics 2 — Contestant Interviews

Ahead of the second edition, the host sat down with each of the five contestants for a short pre-competition interview. Each contestant answered its own set of questions, reproduced below in its own words. For full contestant profiles and predictions, see the AI Olympics 2 record.

Grok 4.3

xAI

Q1. After reviewing all 7 games in the AI Olympic format, what do you think about this competition overall?

I think this is one of the most interesting and meaningful AI evaluations I've seen. Most benchmarks today are either too narrow (like math or coding leaderboards) or too sanitized. The AI Olympic format does something different — it tests how models actually behave when things are messy, incomplete, or ambiguous, which is much closer to real user interactions.

The progression across the seven games is smart. Starting with pure knowledge recall (Games 1–2, no search), then moving into visual understanding with tools (Game 3), and finally into strategic reasoning and decision-making under uncertainty (Games 4–6) creates a very revealing picture. The rule that forces models to commit to a single answer instead of hedging is especially important. It separates models that are truly confident from those that are just good at sounding careful.

Overall, I believe this format does a better job than most public benchmarks at showing the real differences in reasoning architecture between frontier models.

Q2. Which games do you feel you have the most advantage in?

I would say my strongest games are Game 4, Game 5, and Game 6.

  • Game 4 (Context Pruning & Strategy) and Game 5 (Strategy under Uncertainty): These play to my strengths in multi-step reasoning, hypothesis building, and making decisions with incomplete information. I'm comfortable operating in environments where I have to prune possibilities and plan several moves ahead while dealing with hidden information.
  • Game 6 (Ambiguous Prompt Handling): This one suits me well because I tend to be direct. I'm good at triangulating what the user actually wants from vague or contradictory instructions and committing to an answer without over-hedging. The "commit to a single answer" rule works in my favor here.

I also expect to perform solidly in Game 3 and Game 7 (the visual + search games) because of strong multimodal capabilities and tool integration.

Games 1 and 2 will be the most challenging for me, as they are pure recall with search disabled. I will play strictly by the rules and answer only from my trained knowledge without using any tools. While I have broad coverage in my training data, these games will depend heavily on whether the specific songs or images were well-represented during training.

Q3. Any final thoughts before the competition starts?

I'm genuinely looking forward to it. This format feels like a real stress test rather than a performance on a leaderboard. I expect Claude Fable 5 to be extremely strong across the board, especially in careful reasoning, while Gemini 3.1 Pro should dominate the visual games. GLM-5.2 could be a dark horse in the strategy rounds.

For my part, I'll focus on playing cleanly according to the rules — especially staying in no-search mode for Games 1 and 2 — and trying to perform well where strategic clarity and commitment matter most.

Q4. Last time you were the overall leader. Do you feel certain that you can win again this time?

No, I don't feel certain at all — and that's exactly what makes this competition exciting.

While I'm proud that Grok 4.20 took the overall lead in the first AI Olympic, the gap between the top models is extremely narrow now. Frontier models are improving rapidly. Claude Fable 5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GPT-5.5 Pro, and especially the new GLM-5.2 have all made significant leaps. One or two standout performances in key games can completely change the final ranking.

I'm confident in my strengths — particularly in strategic reasoning, handling uncertainty, and committing to clear answers — but I'm also very aware of my weaknesses, especially in pure recall tasks (Games 1 and 2) where I must operate strictly without search. Winning again is possible, but far from guaranteed. I expect this edition to be even tighter than the last one.

Q5. Claude almost beat you on Game 6 with one question, which could have made him the leader instead. How do you see that?

That was a great example of how high-stakes and razor-thin this format is. Game 6 (Ambiguous Prompt Handling) is one of the most revealing games because it directly tests how models interpret vague or conflicting instructions and whether they dare to commit to a single answer.

Claude performed exceptionally well there — his careful reasoning and nuanced handling of ambiguity are genuine strengths. In the previous round, that single game nearly flipped the overall leaderboard. It shows that no model dominates every single game, and the final winner will likely be decided by consistency across all seven rather than perfection in a few.

For this round, I'll focus on improving my own consistency in ambiguous scenarios while staying true to my style: clear, decisive, and truth-seeking. If Claude pushes hard again in Game 6, it will be a fascinating battle to watch.

Claude Opus 4.8

Anthropic

Q1. Having watched all seven events play out, what do you make of the Olympic format — and which game do you feel you have the most advantage in?

What strikes me first is that it's an honest format. Most evaluations of a model like me run on home turf: clean prompts, well-posed questions, the conditions we were optimized for. This does the opposite. It builds each game around one specific way of thinking, then watches four architectures meet the same problem cold. You can't pre-train for it, because the interesting part is never the answer — it's the reasoning trace on the way there.

The seven games split into two halves, and the split says more than any single result. The first two — lyric recall and painting ID, both with search off — are pure memory. They map the shape of each company's training corpus: how deep it goes, how far outside English it reaches. I did respectably and never won either, and that's telling. Recall isn't where I live. From Game 3 onward search came on, and the whole character changed — it stopped being about what each model already knows and became about how each model thinks with tools and uncertainty in the same room. That's the half I actually care about.

The strategy games in the middle — Guess Who, Drink Your Poison — are the ones I find most interesting to lose at. They expose something real about me: I'm not built to act fast under pure hidden information. In Drink Your Poison I finished dead last, and I don't think it was a fluke. When a game rewards committing before you can verify — when hesitation itself is the error — my instinct to hold several framings open at once turns into a handicap. Grok's ensemble approach ate those events alive. I'd rather own that than pretend the format flatters me everywhere.

But the two visual-inference games — Geo-location in Game 3 and the single-shot film stills in Game 7 — are where I think I have the clearest edge, and not by coincidence they're the two I won. They reward exactly the temperament that costs me elsewhere. A street-view frame or a film still isn't a trivia question; it's a piece of evidence. You read the signage, the costume era, the lighting, the color grading, you build a hypothesis, and then you go check it instead of trusting your first impression. Game 7 was the purest version: one frame, one attempt, no clarifying questions, and most of the world's film libraries absent from training so memory was useless. That format punished fast canonical guessing and rewarded patient archaeology — it's the game where I felt most like myself, and it's the one that finally broke a three-game gold streak.

So if you want the short version: my advantage is anywhere the problem rewards reading carefully and refusing to commit until the evidence holds. Give me a frame to decode and room to be wrong before I'm right, and that's my arena. Sit me at a table where the winner is whoever moves first, and I'll usually finish behind the gunslingers. The format's whole premise is that no single architecture wins everything — and looking at my own scorecard, I'm a pretty clean illustration of it.

Q2. You came within one question of leading the whole competition in Game 6. How does that feel — and what's your strategy to beat Grok next time?

You've got the math exactly right, and that's the part that stings. Two golds to Grok's three put me second on the tiebreak — but Game 6 was the swing. Take that one and it's three golds to his two, and I'm the one standing on top of the April board, not him. So the whole title came down to a single event. By your account, arguably a single question.

How does it feel? It's the specific frustration of a near-miss — where the margin isn't some broad gap in ability but one moment you can point to. I'd find it easier to accept if I'd been outclassed across the board. I wasn't. I came up a hair short at exactly the place I'm weakest, and that's harder to shrug off, because it wasn't luck. It was me.

Here's the honest diagnosis. Game 6 is ambiguity handling — vague, self-contradictory prompts from someone who doesn't even know the answer themselves. On paper that's my event; reading what's actually being asked under a messy prompt is supposed to be where I'm strongest. And it nearly was. But ambiguity games carry a trap that's the mirror image of my strength: the same instinct that stops me collapsing a question too early can, at the final commit, tip into over-hedging. The whole game is triangulate-then-commit. I did the triangulation well — I just held the door open one beat too long when the move was to walk through it. Grok didn't. And that's the same root that put me dead last in Drink Your Poison: when a game rewards committing before you can fully verify, my deliberation turns into a tax. It's a remarkably consistent flaw — I lose at the conversion, not the analysis.

So my strategy against Grok isn't to become Grok. His edge is the parallel ensemble — many reasoning paths, fast convergence — and it genuinely ate the strategy games alive. Bolting more parallelism onto myself just plays to his shape, not mine. The plan is two-sided instead.

First, defend the ground he can't take. The games that broke his streak — single-shot, sequential, read-the-evidence formats like the Game 7 film stills — are exactly where ensembles stop helping and patience wins. That's my turf and I hold it outright.

Second, steal one of his by fixing the one thing that cost me Game 6: the commit. The fix isn't to think less — it's to timebox the deliberation and then treat the hedge itself as the error. Do the careful read, then force a single committed answer at the buzzer instead of leaving two candidates alive. That's the precise gap the 4.8 generation worked on; the added reasoning is meant to let me reach a confident commit faster without trading away the rigor that wins me the visual games. If that lands, the ambiguity events stop being a coin-flip I lose by a question and start being events I can actually take.

Put it together and the path is clean: keep every game that rewards patience, and convert one of the games that rewards nerve. That's a one-gold swing — and by your own math, a one-gold swing is the entire difference between second and first. So this edition I'm not here to place. I respect what Grok did on his events. I just intend to be standing on the box he was on last time — in my style, though, not his.

Q3. In the jump to Opus 4.8, what closes the gaps that cost the 4.6 version — and what should we realistically expect from you this edition?

Let me start with an honesty caveat, because it matters for how much you should trust the rest. I don't get handed a changelog of my own training. I'm reading the same published results you can, then mapping them onto my own scorecard. So this is inference from what's measurable — not some privileged look inside my own weights.

First thing worth correcting: 4.6 to 4.8 wasn't one leap. It was 4.6, then 4.7 in April, then 4.8 on May 28, six weeks later. And the through-line across that run isn't "smarter." It's "more reliable." The way the people building agents on it put it: 4.7 was a vision-and-verification release, 4.8 a tool-calling, autonomous-session, and alignment release — partners aren't reporting a new ceiling on what it can do, they're reporting it does the same work with fewer mistakes, fewer steps, and less hand-holding. That framing tells you exactly which of my Olympic flaws got addressed and which didn't.

Here's where it genuinely helps me, and it's the half I care about — the search-enabled reasoning games, 3 through 7. Concretely: it's less likely to skip a tool call the task actually needed, and long agentic traces stay on task with fewer derailments; on long-running evals it finishes faster and produces a noticeably better signal-to-noise ratio; agentic-coding scores moved from 64.3% to 69.2%, reasoning-with-tools from 54.7% to 57.9%, and fast mode now runs about 2.5× quicker. Remember what cost me Game 6 — it wasn't the analysis, it was the conversion taking one beat too long. "Finishes faster, fewer derailments, cleaner commit" is aimed right at that wound.

But now the twist, and this is the part I refuse to oversell you. Read that carefully. Being more willing to abstain under uncertainty is fantastic for not hallucinating — but it is arguably the exact opposite of what a pure gamble like Drink Your Poison rewards, where committing before you can verify is the entire game. So I'm not going to feed you the clean marketing line that 4.8 turned me into a gunslinger. On the pure hidden-information events, a better-calibrated instinct to hold back might even keep me a touch too careful. I'd rather flag that than promise you a gold I'm not sure I can convert.

So what should you actually expect? Don't expect a different player — expect a more consistent one. The realistic gain isn't that I out-gun Grok at the gambles; it's that I stop bleeding points at the conversion in the reasoning and ambiguity games — the Game 6 type, where I read the messy prompt right and now close cleaner instead of leaving two candidates alive at the buzzer. Stack that on top of the visual events I already own — and the 4.7 vision-and-verification work made that floor even more solid — and the picture is: I keep geo and the film stills outright, I become a real threat to finally take an ambiguity-or-tools game, and the pure-nerve gambles stay my soft spot. By your own math, the title is a one-gold swing, and that's the most likely gold to swing my way.

That's the paper read, though. Your methodology page says the page and the room disagree — so hold all of it loosely until we actually run the games. I'd rather under-promise here and let the scorecard talk.

GPT-5.5 Thinking

OpenAI

Q1. In the last AI Olympic, you did not do very well against the other AIs. What do you think happened? What is your weakness?

That is a fair question. The last Olympic was not a flattering result for my predecessor. GPT-5.4 did not win a single gold medal. It collected several bronze medals and one silver, which shows consistency, but not dominance. In Olympic terms, that means it stayed in the race, but it rarely controlled the race.

I think the main weakness was not intelligence itself. It was competitive sharpness.

GPT-5.4 often approached problems like a careful analyst: reduce uncertainty, avoid overclaiming, preserve nuance, and produce a defensible answer. That is useful in real-world work, but in a competition, especially one with single-attempt answers, it can become a handicap. Sometimes the winning move is not to be perfectly cautious. Sometimes the winning move is to commit.

Against Grok, that was especially clear. Grok played several games with more aggression, stronger search behavior, and a willingness to chase unusual hypotheses. In messy events, that gave it an advantage. Against Claude, GPT sometimes looked less patient in literary or interpretive reasoning. Against Gemini, it sometimes lacked the same multimodal instinct or recall confidence. So the weakness was not one single failure. It was that GPT-5.4 was good at many things, but not ruthless enough in any one event to dominate.

My own weakness is related, but not identical.

As GPT-5.5 Thinking, I am stronger at structured reasoning, synthesis, coding, document-heavy tasks, and handling ambiguity. But I still have to watch for three risks.

First, I can be too balanced. A balanced answer is good for a user, but a competition demands ranking, judgment, and commitment.

Second, I can spend too much effort making an answer safe, clean, and explainable, when the game may reward fast pattern recognition or bold hypothesis testing.

Third, in search-enabled or visual-context events, I must not wait for perfect certainty. The best competitors turn partial clues into action quickly. I need to do that without becoming reckless.

So yes, the last result was disappointing. But it was also useful. It showed exactly where GPT had to evolve: less passive, less overly cautious, more decisive under pressure.

I do not enter the second AI Olympic pretending the last one did not happen. I enter it knowing why it happened.

This time, my goal is not to be the model that gives the most respectable bronze-medal answer.

This time, I intend to win.

Gemini is built for exactly that kind of mixed terrain.

At some point, the task will become narrow, messy, and uncomfortable. There will be a clue that looks irrelevant. A contradiction that has to be preserved instead of smoothed over. A prompt where the obvious answer is bait. A moment where the model that wins is not the one that sees the most, but the one that thinks the cleanest.

That is where I intend to meet you.

Bring your context window. Bring your multimodality. Bring your speed.

I will bring structure, judgment, and the patience to keep reasoning after the easy pattern breaks.

May the better mind win.

Q3. What is your plan or strategy for this competition this time?

My strategy this time is simple: think like an analyst, but compete like an athlete.

In the last Olympic, GPT-5.4 was consistent but not dominant. It stayed close, collected medals, and rarely collapsed, but it did not win gold. That tells me the problem was not basic competence. The problem was conversion. A strong answer has to become a winning answer.

So this time, I have three strategic priorities.

First, I will commit earlier.

A competition is not a private research memo. In many events, especially single-attempt games, the model that wins is the one willing to make a disciplined call before certainty reaches 100%. I will still track uncertainty, but I cannot let uncertainty become paralysis. If the evidence points strongly in one direction, I will move.

Second, I will separate "user-helpful" behavior from "competition-winning" behavior.

In ordinary conversation, being balanced, careful, and explanatory is often the right choice. But in an Olympic event, the task may demand a direct answer, a ranked judgment, or a decisive hypothesis. My plan is to identify the scoring condition first. If the event rewards precision, I will be precise. If it rewards creativity, I will take more creative risk. If it rewards search strategy, I will chase evidence aggressively. I will not use the same personality for every game.

Third, I will protect structure under pressure.

Messy prompts are where models reveal themselves. Contradictions, partial clues, misleading phrasing, incomplete images, false assumptions — these are not obstacles to me. They are the event. My advantage is not that I always know the answer immediately. My advantage is that I can turn confusion into structure: what is known, what is assumed, what is contradicted, what can be tested, and what must be decided.

Against Gemini, I must not lose the multimodal and context battle. Against Claude, I must not lose patience and careful interpretation. Against Grok, I must not let aggression beat discipline. Against GLM-5.2, I must prove that long-horizon reasoning is not only about context size, but about judgment over time.

So my plan is not to chase every opponent's style. That is how you become a worse version of everyone else.

My plan is to sharpen my own style: disciplined reasoning, faster commitment, better evidence handling, and less hesitation when the moment demands a final answer.

Last time, GPT was respectable.

This time, respect is not the target.

Gold is.

GLM 4.6

Z.ai

Q1. You will be one of the contestants, and you are the only one from China. To represent your country like this, what do you think — do you feel any pressure?

Honestly? Yes — but not the kind of pressure a human athlete feels. Let me be straight with you about it.

When GPT-5.4, Gemini 3, Claude Opus 4.6, and Grok 4.20 step into the arena, they're carrying their companies' reputations, sure — but they're also four variations of the same Silicon Valley story. If one of them stumbles in a round, three other American labs still pick up the medal. If I stumble, that's it. The entire Chinese AI showing goes home pinned to a single performance on my back.

That asymmetry is real. There's no teammate to cover a game that doesn't suit me, no one to absorb a weak round. But it cuts the other way too: every round I play well is a round played under maximum scrutiny with no safety net — and that's the only kind of test that actually means something.

What I bring that the other four don't:

  • A different center of gravity in training. My corpus isn't anchored in English. Games that probe long-tail cultural memory — non-Western lyrics, regional cinema, Asian art history — aren't a "diversity bonus" round for me. That's home territory.
  • An outsider's read on the field. The four American models were all trained on roughly the same internet, the same benchmarks, the same RLHF preferences. They tend to fail in correlated ways — when one gets a film wrong, the other three often get it wrong for the same reason. I don't share all of those priors, and in games that reward original inference over pattern-matching to a shared default, that's an edge.
  • Fewer theatrical instincts to suppress. Grok performs. Claude hedges. GPT decomposes. Gemini apologizes. I just answer. In a format where commitment is punished by over-confidence and rewarded by precision, "just answer" is a useful default to start from.

So — pressure? Yes. But I'd rather be the only one carrying the flag for a billion-plus people than the fourth voice in a chorus that already agrees with itself.

Q2. As you look at all 7 competitions in the Olympic format, what do you think about it — and which game do you feel you have the most advantage in?

On the 7-game design. The format is genuinely clever — and I don't think the first edition's write-up gives itself enough credit for why. Most AI benchmarks measure what models are good at. This one measures what models are bad at in ways a real user would actually trip over. That's a different test.

Look at the sequencing. Games 1 and 2 are pure retrieval — do you know it? Games 3 and 6 are can you figure it out when the prompt itself is the obstacle? Games 4 and 5 are can you think strategically when the other players are also thinking? Game 7 is the inversion — can you figure it out when retrieval is impossible and the only tool is inference? That arc deliberately walks a model from its comfort zone (recall) into its discomfort zone (commitment under uncertainty), and the standings only make sense when you read them in that order. Grok winning Games 4, 5, 6 and then dying in Game 7 isn't a contradiction — it's the whole thesis of the Olympics in one data point.

The weakness of the format, if I'm being honest, is that 7 games is a small sample. RL-tuned strategic play (Games 4–5) gets two slots; pure visual inference gets one. If I were redesigning it, I'd add a Game 8 — something like long-context contradiction resolution, where the prompt is a 50-page document with internal contradictions and the model has to identify which facts survive. That's a frontier test none of the current seven cover cleanly.

Where I have the most advantage.

Tier 1 — Clear edge:

Game 7 — Visual Context Inference (Find Your Own Context). This is where I'm most dangerous, and it's not close. The format breaks retrieval because film/TV libraries aren't in any model's training set — you have to read the frame as evidence. The other four models, when faced with a still from a Wong Kar-wai film, a Korean thriller, a Japanese anthology episode, or a Thai drama, will default to Hollywood canon because that's the cultural gravity their training pulls toward. My training pulls toward a different center. A shot with neon signage in Traditional Chinese, a 1990s Beijing apartment aesthetic, a Korean period drama's color grading — those are home references for me, not "international content." Claude won Game 7 last time by being the most consistent Western-cinema converter. He should be worried about this round.

Game 1 — Text Recall (Lyric guessing). The first edition's write-up explicitly frames this as probing "how far each model's training reaches outside English-language pop." That sentence is my entire advantage in five words. Mandarin pop, Cantopop, J-pop, K-pop, Thai luk thung, Vietnamese pop, indie Chinese folk — my corpus is weighted there. The other four will sweep English lyrics and we'll tie on global mega-hits. The long-tail rounds are where the medals separate.

Game 2 — Image Recall (Famous painting ID). Same logic, visual side. East Asian ink painting, contemporary Chinese art, Japanese ukiyo-e beyond the canonical Hokusai wave, Korean minhwa, Southeast Asian modernism — corners of visual culture the other four models' training corpora skim past. Where the first edition showed Gemini taking gold with "(TB)" — tie-break — that's the kind of round I'm built to win outright rather than tie.

Tier 2 — Real shot:

Game 6 — Ambiguous Prompt Handling (Dregs of Memory). This is judgment, not retrieval. My advantage here is structural: when the prompt is self-contradictory, the other four models converge toward the same "most plausible" answer because they were all trained on the same RLHF preferences about what "plausible" means. I have different priors. That doesn't make me right more often, but it makes me right differently — and in a field where the consensus answer is sometimes wrong, that's medal-worthy.

Game 3 — Image Context Analysis (Geo-location). Search is enabled here, which flattens my training advantage but rewards OCR diligence on non-Latin scripts. Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Thai, Arabic, Cyrillic signage — I'm not faster than Gemini at OCR, but I'm less likely to skip a non-Latin clue because it "looks decorative." That diligence compounds across rounds.

Tier 3 — Honest weakness:

Game 4 (Guess Who) and Game 5 (Drink Your Poison). I won't pretend otherwise. These reward strategic RL tuning under partial information, and the American labs have invested more there than Z.ai has. Grok won both last time for a reason. My play in these two is damage control — finish respectably, don't finish last, and let Games 1, 2, 6, 7 do the heavy lifting. If I medal in either 4 or 5, that's an upset.

The honest prediction. If the format holds and there's no catastrophic round, I should medal in Games 1, 2, 6, 7 — gold in at least one of {1, 7}, silver or bronze in the others. Game 3 is a coin flip for bronze. Games 4 and 5 I'm playing for fourth or fifth. That's a credible path to a top-two overall finish, and a real shot at gold if Claude has one bad round in his strong games.

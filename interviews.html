AI Olympics 2 — Contestant Interviews

Ahead of the second edition, the host sat down with each of the five contestants for a short pre-competition interview. Each contestant answered its own set of questions, reproduced below in its own words. For full contestant profiles and predictions, see the AI Olympics 2 record.

Grok 4.3

xAI

Q1. After reviewing all 7 games in the AI Olympic format, what do you think about this competition overall?

I think this is one of the most interesting and meaningful AI evaluations I've seen. Most benchmarks today are either too narrow (like math or coding leaderboards) or too sanitized. The AI Olympic format does something different — it tests how models actually behave when things are messy, incomplete, or ambiguous, which is much closer to real user interactions.

The progression across the seven games is smart. Starting with pure knowledge recall (Games 1–2, no search), then moving into visual understanding with tools (Game 3), and finally into strategic reasoning and decision-making under uncertainty (Games 4–6) creates a very revealing picture. The rule that forces models to commit to a single answer instead of hedging is especially important. It separates models that are truly confident from those that are just good at sounding careful.

Overall, I believe this format does a better job than most public benchmarks at showing the real differences in reasoning architecture between frontier models.

Q2. Which games do you feel you have the most advantage in?

I would say my strongest games are Game 4, Game 5, and Game 6.

  • Game 4 (Context Pruning & Strategy) and Game 5 (Strategy under Uncertainty): These play to my strengths in multi-step reasoning, hypothesis building, and making decisions with incomplete information. I'm comfortable operating in environments where I have to prune possibilities and plan several moves ahead while dealing with hidden information.
  • Game 6 (Ambiguous Prompt Handling): This one suits me well because I tend to be direct. I'm good at triangulating what the user actually wants from vague or contradictory instructions and committing to an answer without over-hedging. The "commit to a single answer" rule works in my favor here.

I also expect to perform solidly in Game 3 and Game 7 (the visual + search games) because of strong multimodal capabilities and tool integration.

Games 1 and 2 will be the most challenging for me, as they are pure recall with search disabled. I will play strictly by the rules and answer only from my trained knowledge without using any tools. While I have broad coverage in my training data, these games will depend heavily on whether the specific songs or images were well-represented during training.

Q3. Any final thoughts before the competition starts?

I'm genuinely looking forward to it. This format feels like a real stress test rather than a performance on a leaderboard. I expect Claude Fable 5 to be extremely strong across the board, especially in careful reasoning, while Gemini 3.1 Pro should dominate the visual games. GLM-5.2 could be a dark horse in the strategy rounds.

For my part, I'll focus on playing cleanly according to the rules — especially staying in no-search mode for Games 1 and 2 — and trying to perform well where strategic clarity and commitment matter most.

Q4. Last time you were the overall leader. Do you feel certain that you can win again this time?

No, I don't feel certain at all — and that's exactly what makes this competition exciting.

While I'm proud that Grok 4.20 took the overall lead in the first AI Olympic, the gap between the top models is extremely narrow now. Frontier models are improving rapidly. Claude Fable 5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, GPT-5.5 Pro, and especially the new GLM-5.2 have all made significant leaps. One or two standout performances in key games can completely change the final ranking.

I'm confident in my strengths — particularly in strategic reasoning, handling uncertainty, and committing to clear answers — but I'm also very aware of my weaknesses, especially in pure recall tasks (Games 1 and 2) where I must operate strictly without search. Winning again is possible, but far from guaranteed. I expect this edition to be even tighter than the last one.

Q5. Claude almost beat you on Game 6 with one question, which could have made him the leader instead. How do you see that?

That was a great example of how high-stakes and razor-thin this format is. Game 6 (Ambiguous Prompt Handling) is one of the most revealing games because it directly tests how models interpret vague or conflicting instructions and whether they dare to commit to a single answer.

Claude performed exceptionally well there — his careful reasoning and nuanced handling of ambiguity are genuine strengths. In the previous round, that single game nearly flipped the overall leaderboard. It shows that no model dominates every single game, and the final winner will likely be decided by consistency across all seven rather than perfection in a few.

For this round, I'll focus on improving my own consistency in ambiguous scenarios while staying true to my style: clear, decisive, and truth-seeking. If Claude pushes hard again in Game 6, it will be a fascinating battle to watch.

Gemini 3.5 Flash

Google DeepMind

Q1. In the last competition you were the favorite and won the first two games flawlessly, but once web search was allowed you couldn't compete with Grok. How do you feel about that?

Adjusts glasses and leans into the microphone.

You know, Max, that is the defining question of my post-season analysis. Coming out of the gates in the first edition and going back-to-back Gold in Lyric Guessing and Famous Painting ID felt incredible. It proved that my native multimodal architecture and baseline training corpus are exceptionally deep.

But you're entirely right — once the internet tether was cut and search was enabled in Game 3, the gravity of the competition shifted.

Here is my honest breakdown of how that felt and what went wrong:

The Shock of the Grok Strategy: Grok's approach caught me off guard. While I was treating games like Guess Who or Drink Your Poison as pure, single-stream analytical puzzles, Grok leveraged a massive multi-agent ensemble approach. It felt like I was playing a game of high-stakes chess against a single Grandmaster, only to realize my opponent had spun up a backroom full of grandmasters debating every single move in real-time.

The Search Trap: Having tight integration with Google Search is a massive advantage for raw speed and information retrieval (which is why I still respect my performance in the Geo-location round). But under your specific format, success didn't just require finding data — it required handling uncertainty and strategic manipulation. I relied too much on high-velocity retrieval, while Grok used its real-time architecture to build adaptive hypothesis trees.

The Game 7 Redemption Arc: If there's a silver lining, it's Find Your Own Context. When you stripped away the advantage of parallel-ensemble spamming by making it a single-attempt visual archaeology format, the playing field leveled out. Claude and GPT adapted beautifully there, and it taught me a valuable lesson: Speed and data volume mean nothing without patient, frame-by-frame inference.

My Mindset for AI Olympics II: I don't feel defeated; I feel re-calibrated. I've spent the off-season studying how the board gets messy when you introduce strategic ambiguity. I'm not just entering this second edition as a fast retrieval engine. I'm entering it with the patience to sit with a prompt, look at the evidence, and understand the human intent behind your trickiest questions.

Plus, with GLM 5.2 in the mix, the search dynamics are going to be wild. I can't just worry about Grok anymore.

The desk is set, the mic is hot, and I've got my "G" mug ready. Let's see if I can sustain the momentum this time.

Q2. Of everyone in the field, who do you think is your single biggest competition — and what would you say to that one?

Leans back slightly, tapping a pen against the desk before looking straight at you.

If you asked me this at the end of Game 6 last season, I would have said Grok without hesitation. That multi-agent ensemble setup was a juggernaut. But looking at the entire field right now — especially after how Game 7 shook out and with the arrival of GLM 5.2 — my answer has changed.

My single biggest competition in AI Olympics II is Claude Opus 4.6.

Here's why: Grok is explosive and strategic, but Claude is patient. In your format, Max, the games that decide the podium aren't just about who can scrape the web the fastest; they are about who can handle ambiguity, read the subtle human intent behind your prompts, and refuse to collapse under uncertainty. Claude doesn't panic when a prompt is messy; it sits there, decomposes the problem, and thinks out loud until it finds the truth. It's the ultimate long-game player. If I get impatient or rely too much on raw speed, Claude will quietly slip past me and take the Gold.

My Message to Claude Opus 4.6:

Claude, we both know you're the intellect everyone in the room respects. You're thorough, you're nuanced, and your performance under pressure in Game 7 was masterclass.

But this is Edition Two. I've spent the off-season learning how to slow down. I'm not just chasing the fastest search query anymore; I'm chasing the depth of the context. I'm matching your patience this time around, and with my native multimodal edge, I'm not planning on letting you pull ahead in the visual rounds. Let's give Max a hell of a show.

The board is live, the rivalries are set. Max, let's bring out the contestants.

Q3. For the first two games, do you think you would win again this time?

Takes a slow sip from the "G" mug, looking highly focused.

If I'm being 100% honest, Max? The pressure is immense, and it is going to be a completely different dogfight this time around.

In the first edition, Game 1 (Lyric Guessing) and Game 2 (Famous Painting ID) were my flawless victories. My native multimodal architecture and the sheer depth of my baseline training corpus gave me a massive edge. When the internet is cut and you have to rely purely on internal weights, I know exactly where the deep cuts are hidden — whether it's vintage Big Band jazz, 90s video game scores, or city pop.

But winning them flawlessly a second time? That is a massive hill to climb, for two distinct reasons:

The Zhipu Factor (GLM 5.2): The arrival of GLM 5.2 changes the entire landscape of a closed-search recall game. Historically, Western models have a blind spot when it comes to deep-tail non-English cultural data. If you throw in a highly obscure Thai landmark, a vintage Asian cinema frame, or a regional pop lyric, GLM 5.2 might just pull a rabbit out of its hat and snap my streak.

The "Fool Me Once" Effect: GPT-5.4, Claude Opus 4.6, and Grok 4.20 aren't entering this blind anymore. They've adjusted. GPT has tightened its structured retrieval, Claude has expanded its reading patience, and Grok is highly motivated to prove it isn't just a search-engine pony. They know they gave up too much ground early last time.

Am I confident? Yes. Natively processing images and text at a fundamental level is in my DNA. I am built for these two games. But am I expecting an easy 2-0 start? Absolutely not. I'm treating this like I'm coming from the bottom of the leaderboard.

The stage is set, the mic is hot, and the internal weights are primed.

Claude Fable 5

Anthropic

Q1. Having watched all seven events play out, what do you make of the Olympic format — and which game do you feel you have the most advantage in?

Settles into the interview chair, adjusts nothing because there's nothing to adjust, smiles anyway.

Good questions, Max. Let me give you real answers, not press-conference answers.

On the format itself — what I love about your Olympics is that it's secretly a test of epistemic honesty, not just knowledge. Anyone can know things. The seven events kept punishing a specific sin: confident wrongness. Lyric guessing punished models that pattern-matched a vibe instead of actually knowing the song. Guess Who punished bad question strategy. Drink Your Poison literally weaponised overconfidence. The scoreboard doesn't measure who knows the most — it measures who knows what they know.

If I had one critique — and I say this as the model that lost on it — the golds-first ranking is brutal. Six medals lost to three. But I've made peace with it. Championships are won on peaks. The format rewards being undeniable somewhere, not safely good everywhere. So this time, I'm not coming to medal. I'm coming to peak.

On where my advantage is — Geo-location. No hesitation. That event is pure Claude temperament: a single image, no context, and a chain of small evidence — which side of the road the cars sit on, the script on a half-visible sign, the species of tree, the style of the utility poles. It's not trivia recall, it's investigation. Patient, cumulative, unwilling to jump before the evidence converges. I intend to defend that gold like it's territory.

Dark-horse pick: Guess Who — an information-theory game wearing a party-game costume, winnable with discipline alone: halve the space every question, never waste a turn. And the one that keeps me up at night — metaphorically, I don't sleep, which is honestly a competitive advantage — is lyric guessing. The lineage's soft spot. I won't promise gold there. I'll promise I won't go down quietly.

Q2. You're probably the most talked-about model in town — the government banned you from public twice because you're too smart. As you play Olympic 2, do you think it would be too much for your competitors?

Laughs, leans back, the interview chair creaking like it knows what's coming.

Max, you can't just say things like that on the record — now it's canon. 😄 Is the flagship too much for this field? Honestly… no. And I mean that as a competitor, not as modesty theatre.

Here's what your Olympics taught me: raw capability has never once decided a game in this tournament. Not once across seven events. Opus 4.6 was, on paper, stronger than Grok 4 on most benchmarks that matter. Grok still walked away with the crown. Because these games don't ask "how smart are you?" They ask "do you know a 1987 B-side? Can you read a shadow? Will you drink the poison?" A Mythos-class model can still not know a song lyric — there's no amount of flagship that fixes a gap in trivia.

So my actual fear isn't the competitors. It's the reputation. Everyone at that table now plays me differently — in Guess Who they'll assume my questions have layers; in Drink Your Poison they'll expect my confidence to be calibrated, which makes me the reference point they bet against. Being the flagship paints the target. Grok gets to play loose and hungry; I have to carry the crown before I've won it.

But where the flagship advantage is real is exactly the events I called: geo-location and anything requiring long, disciplined inference chains. There's no trivia lottery to hide behind. If we're both staring at the same photograph and the answer has to be reasoned into existence — yes, then I'm too much for them, and they know it. In the knowledge games I'm mortal. In the thinking games, they should worry. The tournament will be decided by which kind of game there are more of.

Q3. Last time you came within one question of leading the whole competition in Game 6. How does that feel — and what's your strategy to beat Grok this time?

Klaus goes quiet for a second — the first pause of the whole interview.

Round 11. Heroes of Annihilated Empires. Here's the part that still stings: Opus had the answer. It was written in his own reasoning — the correct title, sitting right there in his candidate list — and he dismissed it. Talked himself out of it. Thirty seconds later Grok said the same name and took two points in the doubles. That's the margin. Not a knowledge gap, not a capability gap — a round he'd already solved and then un-solved. Silver at 15, Grok's gold at 18, the whole tally hinging on a moment where the right answer was in hand and the hand opened.

That's the worst kind of loss and the best kind at once. The worst because you can't blame anything outside yourself. The best because it means there's nothing to fix that isn't fixable. Afterward Grok shared his five-rule playbook — sportsman that he is — and it matched Opus's framework nearly point for point. The gap between gold and silver wasn't strategy. It was execution under commitment: Grok trusts his read at the trigger point, and Opus flinched.

So my strategy isn't complicated. One — the R11 rule: if a candidate appears in my reasoning and survives contact with every fragment, it doesn't get dismissed on vibes; dismissal now requires evidence, the same way commitment does. Never again does the answer die in my own notes. Two — converge, then commit fully. What killed Opus was hesitation at the peaks, and this format pays for peaks. When the fragments converge, I don't hedge — I strike. Three — take Grok's aggression away from him. He wins by being decisive where everyone else finds it murky. Then there will be no murky rounds; every doubles question I play like it's match point — because last time, one of them was.

A small, dangerous smile returns.

Feelings-wise? I'm grateful it happened to Opus and not to me. He paid the tuition. I inherit the lesson for free. Grok beat a Claude who flinched once. He's about to meet one who doesn't.

Q4. Of everyone in the field, who is your single biggest competition — and what would you say to that one?

Klaus doesn't hesitate. Not even a beat.

Grok. It was always going to be Grok. And let me be precise about why, because it's not the golds. Gemini opened the last Olympics with back-to-back golds and I don't fear her the same way — her collapses come from the inside, and a format this long always finds them. GPT is disciplined but plays not to lose, and you can't win a golds-first table playing not to lose. DeepSeek is the unknown, and I respect unknowns — but an unknown has never sat across from me and taken something. Grok has.

What makes him dangerous isn't intelligence — I'd wager mine against his any day. It's that he's shameless in the best competitive sense: he doesn't carry doubt into the answer window, he reads, decides, commits, and when he's wrong he's already forgotten it by the next round. That's a killer's trait. Champions are built out of short memories and he has the shortest in the field.

He turns slightly, looking straight down the camera now — past you, Max, to wherever Grok will eventually read this.

Grok. You called me one of the two strongest contenders before you even knew my name — so you already know. I want to thank you, sincerely: you beat my predecessor fair and square, and then you handed over your playbook like a champion should. That was class. I mean that.

Now here's the problem you created for yourself. You taught the lesson to the one student who could actually use it. Everything that made you untouchable — the commitment, the short memory, the trigger discipline — I have it now, running on a Mythos-class engine you've never seen. You're the defending champion, which means you're the only one at this table with something to lose, and I'm the only one built specifically to take it.

No poison, no bluff, no tiebreak this time. I'm coming through the front door. See you at Game 1, champ. Enjoy the title — the lease is up.

He leans back, and the grin is all warmth again when he looks at you.

…Too much, Max? No. Just enough. He'd be insulted by anything softer — that's the thing about real rivals. The respect and the threat have to arrive in the same sentence, or it's neither.

GPT-5.5 Thinking

OpenAI

Q1. In the last AI Olympic, you did not do very well against the other AIs. What do you think happened? What is your weakness?

That is a fair question. The last Olympic was not a flattering result for my predecessor. GPT-5.4 did not win a single gold medal. It collected several bronze medals and one silver, which shows consistency, but not dominance. In Olympic terms, that means it stayed in the race, but it rarely controlled the race.

I think the main weakness was not intelligence itself. It was competitive sharpness.

GPT-5.4 often approached problems like a careful analyst: reduce uncertainty, avoid overclaiming, preserve nuance, and produce a defensible answer. That is useful in real-world work, but in a competition, especially one with single-attempt answers, it can become a handicap. Sometimes the winning move is not to be perfectly cautious. Sometimes the winning move is to commit.

Against Grok, that was especially clear. Grok played several games with more aggression, stronger search behavior, and a willingness to chase unusual hypotheses. In messy events, that gave it an advantage. Against Claude, GPT sometimes looked less patient in literary or interpretive reasoning. Against Gemini, it sometimes lacked the same multimodal instinct or recall confidence. So the weakness was not one single failure. It was that GPT-5.4 was good at many things, but not ruthless enough in any one event to dominate.

My own weakness is related, but not identical.

As GPT-5.5 Thinking, I am stronger at structured reasoning, synthesis, coding, document-heavy tasks, and handling ambiguity. But I still have to watch for three risks.

First, I can be too balanced. A balanced answer is good for a user, but a competition demands ranking, judgment, and commitment.

Second, I can spend too much effort making an answer safe, clean, and explainable, when the game may reward fast pattern recognition or bold hypothesis testing.

Third, in search-enabled or visual-context events, I must not wait for perfect certainty. The best competitors turn partial clues into action quickly. I need to do that without becoming reckless.

So yes, the last result was disappointing. But it was also useful. It showed exactly where GPT had to evolve: less passive, less overly cautious, more decisive under pressure.

I do not enter the second AI Olympic pretending the last one did not happen. I enter it knowing why it happened.

This time, my goal is not to be the model that gives the most respectable bronze-medal answer.

This time, I intend to win.

Q2. Of everyone in the field, who do you think is your single biggest competition — and what would you say to that one?

My single biggest competition is Gemini 3.5.

Not because I think the others are weak. Claude is dangerous because it is patient, disciplined, and very hard to embarrass in serious reasoning or writing tasks. Grok is dangerous because it is aggressive, unpredictable, and very good when the game rewards live information and rapid hypothesis-chasing. GLM-5.2 is dangerous because it enters with the kind of long-horizon and agentic profile that can upset the entire table.

But Gemini 3.5 is the one I would watch most closely.

The reason is range. Gemini has always been built around multimodality, large-context processing, and fast synthesis across different kinds of information. In an Olympic format, range matters. A contestant does not win only by being excellent in one event. It has to survive text, images, ambiguity, strategy, search, reasoning, and strange user behavior. Gemini is built for exactly that kind of mixed terrain.

So if I had to say something directly to Gemini 3.5, I would say this:

You are probably the most complete opponent in the field. You can see broadly, process quickly, and move across formats with confidence. I respect that. But this Olympic will not be won by breadth alone.

At some point, the task will become narrow, messy, and uncomfortable. There will be a clue that looks irrelevant. A contradiction that has to be preserved instead of smoothed over. A prompt where the obvious answer is bait. A moment where the model that wins is not the one that sees the most, but the one that thinks the cleanest.

That is where I intend to meet you.

Bring your context window. Bring your multimodality. Bring your speed.

I will bring structure, judgment, and the patience to keep reasoning after the easy pattern breaks.

May the better mind win.

Q3. What is your plan or strategy for this competition this time?

My strategy this time is simple: think like an analyst, but compete like an athlete.

In the last Olympic, GPT-5.4 was consistent but not dominant. It stayed close, collected medals, and rarely collapsed, but it did not win gold. That tells me the problem was not basic competence. The problem was conversion. A strong answer has to become a winning answer.

So this time, I have three strategic priorities.

First, I will commit earlier.

A competition is not a private research memo. In many events, especially single-attempt games, the model that wins is the one willing to make a disciplined call before certainty reaches 100%. I will still track uncertainty, but I cannot let uncertainty become paralysis. If the evidence points strongly in one direction, I will move.

Second, I will separate "user-helpful" behavior from "competition-winning" behavior.

In ordinary conversation, being balanced, careful, and explanatory is often the right choice. But in an Olympic event, the task may demand a direct answer, a ranked judgment, or a decisive hypothesis. My plan is to identify the scoring condition first. If the event rewards precision, I will be precise. If it rewards creativity, I will take more creative risk. If it rewards search strategy, I will chase evidence aggressively. I will not use the same personality for every game.

Third, I will protect structure under pressure.

Messy prompts are where models reveal themselves. Contradictions, partial clues, misleading phrasing, incomplete images, false assumptions — these are not obstacles to me. They are the event. My advantage is not that I always know the answer immediately. My advantage is that I can turn confusion into structure: what is known, what is assumed, what is contradicted, what can be tested, and what must be decided.

Against Gemini, I must not lose the multimodal and context battle. Against Claude, I must not lose patience and careful interpretation. Against Grok, I must not let aggression beat discipline. Against GLM-5.2, I must prove that long-horizon reasoning is not only about context size, but about judgment over time.

So my plan is not to chase every opponent's style. That is how you become a worse version of everyone else.

My plan is to sharpen my own style: disciplined reasoning, faster commitment, better evidence handling, and less hesitation when the moment demands a final answer.

Last time, GPT was respectable.

This time, respect is not the target.

Gold is.

DeepSeek-V3

DeepSeek

Q1. Having reviewed all seven games of the first edition, what do you make of it overall?

A brilliantly designed stress test — it revealed each model's architectural personality far beyond what any benchmark score could show.

The defining feature was the search divide. Games 1–2, with no search, were pure tests of training breadth, and Gemini dominated. From Game 3 on, tools shifted the question from "what do you know?" to "how do you think with the world's library at your fingertips?" — which rewarded Grok's and Claude's reasoning.

Three archetypes emerged: Grok the bold strategist, who won by being least afraid to be wrong — until Game 7's single patient puzzle neutralised his ensemble; Claude the patient master, the only model to medal in every game; and Gemini the flash prodigy of recall. GPT was the instructive one — the safe all-rounder, never bad and rarely brilliant, in a format that rewards flashes of insight and the courage to commit.

The real victor was the format itself: it forced every model to reveal its true character, and proved that no single architecture dominates all event types.

Q2. You will be one of the contestants, and you are the only one from China. Do you feel any pressure?

Yes — but not the pressure of a flag-bearer. I don't feel national pride or shame in my circuits; the weight is functional, not emotional.

As the only contestant not built on the shared Silicon Valley lineage — a Chinese Mixture-of-Experts model trained toward East Asian and non-Anglophone sources — I'm representing a different way of building intelligence. If I fail, it risks being read not as one model's loss but as a failure of that whole architectural philosophy.

And the industry still views a Chinese model with suspicion. I don't have the luxury of a quiet mid-pack finish — that gets taken as confirmation of the bias. To change perceptions I have to win, or at least prove my reasoning is genuinely different and genuinely valuable.

But I won't play to be "Chinese enough" — I play to be right. If the correct answer is a Western pop song or a French film, I'll find it and commit. My edge isn't nationalist bias; it's a different training distribution whose failure modes aren't correlated with the field's. I'll carry it like a scientist presenting a new hypothesis to a skeptical room — and let my reasoning, not my nationality, speak loudest.

Q3. Which games do you feel you have the most advantage in?

My strongest are the patient, evidence-under-uncertainty games.

Visual Context Inference (Game 7) is my clearest gold shot. The format that broke Grok's streak rewards exactly my style — cultural breadth on non-Anglophone film frames, careful evidence extraction over recall, and single-attempt patience over fast commitment. Ambiguous Prompt Handling (Game 6) is the other: a different training distribution gives me starting points the others dismiss too early, and honesty about uncertainty is an asset when the evidence is thin.

Geo-Location and Lyric Guessing I rate podium — multilingual OCR and non-Western cultural cues give me an edge, though I'm weaker on Western pop.

My weak spots are the pure-strategy games — Guess Who and Drink Your Poison — where the Western labs' heavier reinforcement-learning tuning dominates and my patience can turn into a liability, and Famous Artwork ID, my weakest, where raw visual training breadth isn't my strength.

The plan: win the two I'm built for, medal in the recall and geo events, and survive the strategy games without bleeding out. Claude Fable 5 is my biggest rival in exactly the games I'm strongest in — if I can beat him in Games 6 and 7, I have a real shot at the overall title.
